## 4.1 Research Environment and Respondents

This study is conducted in a fully computational research environment. It does not draw on a human population but instead studies typographic artifacts. The units of analysis are therefore not people but *fonts* and the *rendered text-crop images* derived from them. Because the study addresses typographic hallucination in generative AI images, no clean corpus of such crops exists at the scale a metric-learning model requires. The environment is consequently a *synthetic-data* one: the primary dataset is generated by the researchers from a curated, open-source font palette rather than downloaded ready-made. This follows the precedent of DeepFont, which likewise built a targeted synthetic dataset of rendered glyphs to train a deep visual font recognizer (Wang et al., 2015).

### Digital Source and Trusted Environment

The font palette is sourced from Google Fonts (https://fonts.google.com; source repository https://github.com/google/fonts), the open-source typeface library maintained by Google LLC. Google Fonts is a trusted environment for three reasons. First, its faces are released under permissive open licenses (predominantly the SIL Open Font License), so every typeface can be freely rendered, degraded, and redistributed without the licensing constraints that encumber commercial font catalogues. Second, the collection is curated and version-controlled, giving each typeface a stable identity and provenance that a reproducible dataset requires. Third, its faces are among the most widely deployed on the web, so a model trained on them maps hallucinated text back onto a realistic, free reconstruction palette. From this library the study selects the top 50 to 100 Google Fonts, drawn from the platform's own popularity ordering and chosen to span the four structural family classes (serif, sans-serif, display, and monospace) established in Section 3.2.1.

The decision to bound the palette this tightly is deliberate, and it follows from both the framework's design and the evidence on large catalogues. Conceptually, the system is built for open-set recognition, in which the known set is intentionally finite and every out-of-palette input is routed to an "unknown" verdict; because the space of possible fonts is effectively unbounded and cannot be enumerated (Lu et al., 2025), a localized known set is the premise of the design rather than a limitation of it. Empirically, scaling the catalogue buys coverage, not robustness. The 3,474-font Storia-AI classifier is the field's largest reference embedding, yet it still overfits pristine glyphs and degrades under generative deformation (Jiang et al., 2025), and Chen et al. (2026) show that pushing to 394 variants drops family-level accuracy to 40.2%, because the additional fonts are largely near-identical weight variants whose confusion dominates the error matrix. A curated palette that omits redundant near-duplicates avoids that failure mode directly. The trimmed set also excludes the typographic long tail of rare and display faces, which is both the hardest to recognize and the most severely hallucinated by diffusion models (Zhuang et al., 2025), and it keeps per-class image density high for the metric-learning objective, since a fixed rendering budget spread over fewer classes yields more examples per font. Finally, the study's reconstruction goal only requires the faces designers actually reuse, which the popularity ordering already surfaces.

A second, external repository defines the study's comparative baseline: Storia-AI's Google Font Classifier (the `font-classify/` sandbox), a pre-trained classifier over 3,474 Google Fonts. The classifier was chosen because it is the field's reference embedding for font identity, adopted as the fidelity backbone in recent typographic work (Jiang et al., 2025). Benchmarking against it lets the study demonstrate, on a recognized standard, how weights fitted to pristine glyphs degrade under generative-AI deformation.

### Respondents: The Entities Being Studied

Because the data concern non-human objects, there are no human respondents in the primary sense. The entities being studied are of two linked kinds:

1. **Fonts (typefaces).** The 50 to 100 selected Google Fonts are the population of typographic identities the system must recognize. Each font is one class label in the recognition problem and is characterized by the measurable structural traits defined in Chapter 3 (x-height, terminal form, stroke contrast, and counter shape).

2. **Rendered text-crop images.** From each font the pipeline renders pristine word crops and then applies the algorithmic degradation operator of Section 3.2.3 to produce hallucinated variants. These grayscale image crops (standardized to a fixed input resolution, detailed in Section 4.3.1) are the individual "profiles" the model actually consumes; each degraded crop is an instance whose ground-truth label is the font it was rendered from.

The **unit of analysis is the isolated text crop**, not the full image. A real deployment receives a whole generative-AI image, which may contain several text regions in different fonts, so an upstream step must first locate and crop each region. That localization is handled by a standard off-the-shelf text detector and is treated as a delimitation rather than a contribution: this study does not train, tune, or evaluate the localizer, and it reports no detection metrics. Every crop the localizer emits is classified independently by the recognition engine, which is where the thesis's contribution lies. Fixing the crop as the unit of analysis keeps the dataset, the metrics of Section 4.2, and the human-proxy baseline all defined at the same level.

A small human element enters only at the evaluation stage, and strictly as a benchmark rather than a training population: a three-person consensus panel establishes a human-proxy ground truth over a held-out set of 100 actual generative-AI image text boxes. This panel provides the accuracy floor against which the automated system is compared (elaborated in Section 4.10); its composition and screening criteria are specified there. Because the raters are practicing typography and design professionals for whom a synchronous, face-to-face labeling session would be impractical to convene, the panel operates in a **remote, asynchronous online environment**: each rater completes the rating exercise independently through a web-based form, at their own pace and without a shared session or contact between raters. The distributed setup also preserves rater independence by construction, since no rater can observe another's judgments before submitting, and it keeps the environment reproducible because every rater receives the identical instrument over the identical crop set. The administration of that form is detailed in Section 4.2.3. Apart from this proxy panel, the study's respondents are entirely typographic artifacts.
