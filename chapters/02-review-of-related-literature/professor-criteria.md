
Relevance of Related Literatures
It provides a critical summary and synthesis of existing studies, theories, and scholarly works relevant to your research topic. 
Its purpose is to establish the academic foundation of your study by 
showing what has already been discovered (what is known), 
identifying gaps or limitations in current knowledge (what is missing),  and
positioning your research within the broader scholarly conversation (why your study matters).
It does this by:
Presenting theories and concepts that frame your research.
Summarizing and comparing findings from past studies.
Highlighting gaps, contradictions, or underexplored areas.
Justifying the need for your own investigation.

Key Points
Uses APA in-text citations (Author, year). (e.g. 
Examples: (Johnson & Brown, 2020), Garcia and Lee (2021)
Shows agreement and contrast among findings.
Agreement (positive findings): Johnson & Brown (2020), Smith (2019), Garcia & Lee (2021) → All highlight benefits of mobile learning (engagement, retention, flexibility).
Contrast (negative findings): Martinez (2018) → Points out risks (distractions, reduced interaction).
Synthesizes multiple studies instead of listing them one by one.
The paragraph doesn’t just list studies—it shows how they converge on benefits but diverge on risks, leading to a justified research gap.
Ends with a research gap or need for further study.
The gap lies in the lack of research on how to balance the advantages of mobile learning with its potential downsides.

Key Points (APA)
Each entry is alphabetized by the first author’s last name.
Italicize book/journal titles.
Include DOIs or URLs when available.
Hanging indent format is required in APA (the second line of each reference is indented).

Step-by-Step Guide to Synthesis
Gather Relevant Sources
Collect studies, articles, or theories related to your topic.
Identify Common Themes
Look for agreements (similar findings) and contrasts (different or opposing findings).
Example:
Agreement → Mobile apps improve engagement and retention.
Contrast → Risk of distraction and reduced interaction.
Group Sources by Theme
Organize them into clusters (e.g., benefits vs. drawbacks) to prevent a “study-by-study” summary and creates a narrative.
Write Integrative Sentences
Combine multiple sources in one sentence to show agreement.
Example: Several studies agree that mobile learning enhances student engagement and knowledge retention (Johnson & Brown, 2020; Smith, 2019; Garcia & Lee, 2021).
Use transition words to highlight contrast.
Example: In contrast, Martinez (2018) cautioned that excessive reliance on mobile devices may lead to distractions.
Conclude with a Research Gap
After showing agreement and contrast, point out what is missing.
Example: Despite these findings, limited research explores strategies to balance the benefits of mobile learning with its potential drawbacks.

Paragraph Template
Sentence 1 – Introduce the theme: 

Begin with a general statement about the topic or theme you’re reviewing. 
Example: Mobile learning has been widely studied as a tool for improving student engagement and accessibility in education.
Sentence 2 – Show agreement (multiple sources together): 

Summarize findings from two or more studies that support the same idea. 
Example: Several researchers agree that mobile applications enhance motivation and knowledge retention (Lopez, 2017; Chen & Rivera, 2018).

Sentence 3 – Provide specific examples (still agreement): 


Add detail from one or two studies to illustrate the point. 
Example: Lopez (2017) found that gamified apps increased motivation in science classes, while Chen and Rivera (2018) noted improved access for rural students.

Sentence 4 – Show contrast (different or opposing findings): 


Introduce studies that highlight limitations or challenges. 
Example: In contrast, Patel (2019) argued that mobile apps may shorten attention spans, and Nguyen (2020) emphasized the need for teacher guidance to ensure effectiveness.

Sentence 5 – Synthesize and identify the gap: 


Bring agreement and contrast together, then point out what is missing. 
Example: This agreement on benefits, alongside contrasting concerns about drawbacks, reveals a gap in the literature: few studies have examined strategies to maximize the advantages of mobile learning while minimizing its negative effects.

Key Considerations for Choosing
Word Processor Integration
Ensure the software offers plugins for your preferred writing environment (e.g., Microsoft Word, Google Docs, or LaTeX).
Storage Constraints
Free cloud storage varies significantly; Zotero offers 300 MB for free, whereas Mendeley and EndNote Online provide 2GB. Consider your need to sync large libraries of PDFs.
Institutional Access
Check with your university library—many institutions offer premium site-licenses or institutional accounts (especially for EndNote).
