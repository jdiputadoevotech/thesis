Chapter 2: Review of Related Literature
2.1 Introduction
This chapter reviews the literature defining the scope of this thesis and introduces the components of the proposed solution. The review is organized around the structure of the problem itself. It first establishes the limits of current approaches, describes the phenomenon that undermines them, and then introduces the components needed to resolve these limitations. Five thematic areas race this progression. Legacy font recognition, OCR/Layout and Closed-Set Vector assumptions reviews visual font recognition, the paradigm this work challenges. Traditional methods including DeepFont, classical OCR, Layout analysis, and attention-based localization rely on a closed-set, rigid-glyph assumption. However, generative AI text violates this assumption. Generative AI typographic hallucinations and diffusion latent mutations details these violations, specifically the micro warping, variable kerning, and cross-character attention smearing that this thesis terms typographic hallucination. Existing generative AI research only studies these deformations to suppress them inside the generator, rather than to recover typeface identity from the deformed output. The next three themes build the proposed recognizer. Logit-space thresholds and open-set rejection scores describe the open-set rejection machinery that labels out of palette crops as “unknown”. Feature disentanglement and Frozen-ViT Metric Embedding details the disentangled frozen-ViT embedding used to calculate the rejection score. Advanced Visual Distance Metrics (SSIM) and Human-Proxy Ground-Truth Alignment introduces the deformation-robust distance metric and calibrated Top-K measurement layer used to validate the pipeline.


2.2 Legacy Font Recognition, OCR/Layout & Closed-Set Vector Assumptions
Visual font recognition as a discipline has been studied for a while now, with traces to Wang, Yang, Jein, Shechtman, Aseem and Huang’s (2015) DeepFont with Adobe Research. It was the first, large-scale deep convulational system for identifying typefaces from photographic inputs. DeepFont’s innovations were inseparable from the assumptions that were built into it. It trained on a synthetic set of clean typographic specimens generated directly from font files, guaranteeing that the discriminative features the network learned were uncontaminated by rendering artifacts like noise. It learned to read the typeface, relating to its stroke ratios, serif geometry, terminal curvature and ascender-to-x-height proportions. It was evaluated under the Top-1/Top-3 (TopK) accuracy protocol that remains the discipline’s standard. It proved that a network trained on clean glyphs, can generalize real photographic inputs with useful accuracy. However this assumption of clean glyphs means that every input is presumed to contain a glyph which exists at render time, and recognition is the recovery of that “fixed” identity from an environmental degradation channel, such as pictures or documents. We adopt the Top-K protocol from DeepFont because it is the established standard, in a system where there is no clean glyph to recover, because signal and noise co-emerge from the same generative process.

The same assumption asserts itself in contemporary OCR and layout systems such as by Ponnuru et al. (2024) report 98% accuracy for a Tesseract-based prescription digitization pipeline under scanning defects, and closed-boundary layout analysis grows stable for bounding regions from coherent glyph interiors (Bhunia et al., 2018). Both systems implicitly compute for the same function, that function is well-posed only when an existing, antecedent glyph exists. It is undefined in this sense where the glyph in the image is not a degraded copy of a vector mastery but the primary output of a system with no access entirely to the intended typeface’s geometry.

Recent shifts in document layout analysis that includes Wang, Lv, et al.’s (2025) TransTab and Sheikh et al.’s (2024) UnSupDLA, have begun replacing rigid geometric priors with attention-based, layout-independent feature extraction, establishing a direct precedent for the unsupervised, frozen-ViT architecture adoption. However, while these new attempts to relax spatial constraints during localization, they leave the morphological assumptions of font identity untouched. 
Methodologically, we can draw the theoretical scaffolding from Sarker’s (2021) deep-learning taxonomy to justify a lightweight metric head over a frozen backbone, and Plastropoulos and Tegos’s (2024) framework to license synthetic data augmentation via geometric and typographic degradation. Yet, because the broader literature, stretching from DeepFont’s foundational closed-set baseline (Wang et al., 2015) to modern attention-driven models as mentioned above, presumes topologically faithful glyphs or treats distortion merely as an external noise channel. None of these studies actually address whether font identity can be recovered from a glyph whose structural deformation is not noise to be inverted, but an irreducible, probabilistic property of a generative latent model.


2.3 Generative AI Typographic Hallucinations & Diffusion Latent Mutations
AI producing morphologically unreliable text is established by several literature studies. Liu et al. (2024) diagnosed the visual text rendering in Stable Diffusion XL, traces the failure to an architectural limitation: the standard text encoder for AI treats a character sequence as a semantic unit and lacks the sub-character spatial awareness needed for typographically accurate output. This resulted in fewer than 20% of uncorrected SDXL renderings as accurate. The study also proposes their own Glyph-ByT5 correction, which fine-tunes the encoder on glyph-conditioned data, and raises the accuracy to 90%, confirming the failure is architectural rather than fundamental. The fact is that for an uncorrected baseline, deformation is the default output state.

Li, Song, et al. (2025) figured out that state-of-the-art vision-language models (VLMs) are readily derailed by an effect: The “Stroop-type” semantic inference, in which the lexical meaning of the rendered word overrides the model’s reading of the visual typeface. Because this effect operates even on clean, legible text, semantic distraction and glyph deformation are independently sufficient to defeat general-purpose recognition. We close off off-the-shelf VLMs as a solution and create a dedicated visual-texture pipeline architecturally isolated from this lexical content.

Generative text deformation arises within the generator's attention structure, where cross-character attention bleeding causes micro-warping, variable kerning, and glyph blending (Gillani et al., 2025), alongside multi-instance typographic entanglement (Du et al., 2025). Crucially, this degradation concentrates in the typographic long tail (display, script, and rare faces), forcing a recognizer to exercise caution exactly where a font’s clean profile would otherwise inspire confidence (Zhuang et al., 2025). Furthermore, because diffusion models map style onto a continuous latent manifold rather than discrete catalogs, generative text yields morphologically novel glyphs sampled between known typographies (Kondo et al., 2024). While existing evaluation frameworks like VTPBench quantify these errors (Shu et al., 2025), the broader literature studies deformation exclusively to suppress it within the generator using character-centric metrics (CER/WER). However, none of these studies treats this documented deformation distribution as an inevitable input distribution to evaluate whether the underlying typeface identity remains recoverable.

2.4 Logit-Space Thresholds & Open-Set Rejection Scores
To bridge the gap between closed-set limitations and irregular generative outputs, we must establish an inferential system required for open-set rejection; rejecting out-of-palette hallucinations to an “unknown” bin rather than forcing an inaccurate classification. There are two studies that highlight a post-hoc layer approach: Hofmann et al.’s (2024) Energy-based Hopfield Boosting (EHB) and Djurisic et al.’s (2024) Logic Scaling (LTS). Hofmann et al.’s (2024) approach utilizes an energy formalism to isolate manifold outliers, while Djurisic et al.’s (2024) introduces architecture-agnostic magnitude normalization, crucial for domains lacking explicit out-of-distribution (OOD) training data. While Lu et al.’s (2025) survey validates these training-agnostic approaches as the only viable choice against an infinite out-of-palette baseline, there is a clear gap. Existing benchmarks evaluate these approaches exclusively on natural-image datasets (e.g, ImageNet, CIFAR) featuring course, object-level semantic differences, leaving the effectiveness of these methods unproved when applied to the micro-irregularities of generative font deformation.


2.5 Feature Disentanglement & Frozen-ViT Metric Embedding
Providing robust input for the rejection mechanism is highly important. A study by Wang et al., (2024) describes a representation layer that produces disentangled, axis-aligned features, making typographic identity mathematically orthogonal to deformation artifacts. The architecture uses a frozen, register-corrected DINOv2 backbone (Oquab et al., 2024; Darcet et al., 2024) to extract fine-grained patch features, bypassing domain-specific fine-tuning. To prevent “Stroop-type” interference, the approach uses trait-localized prompts (Chowdhury et al., 2025) and label-free prompt distillation (Li, Li, et al., 2024) to direct attention to letterform anatomy. This establishes a continuous metric space (Huang & Zhou, 2022) where proximity ranking replaces categorical assignment letting the model distinguish genuine typographic variance from hallucinatory corruption. The methods above work on clean typographic or natural-image benchmarks, but we do not know if frozen attention maps can localize anatomical strokes under the severe warping, smearing, and character bleeding common in diffusion-generated glyphs.


2.6 Advanced Visual Distance Metrics (SSIM) & Human-Proxy Ground-Truth Alignment
Evaluating output quality against an empirical standard presents a measurement layer that pairs deformation-robust structural distance with dynamically calibrated confidence sets. Because pixel-level metrics collapse under spatial shifts in generative outputs, a system with DeepSSIM (Zhang et al., 2024) and SAMScore (Li, Chen, et al., 2025) in a re-render-and-compare loop is highly suggested. This loop evaluates typographic structure using embeddings from deep feature spaces and region topologies, a method supported by continuous structural distance optimization (Umer et al., 2022). To turn classifier probabilities into reliable uncertainty claims, this work uses Rank-Calibrated Class-Conditional Conformal Prediction (RC3P) (Shi et al., 2024) and its prevalence-adjusted long-tail extension (Ding et al., 2025) to generate input-dependent prediction sets with formal mathematical coverage guarantees. While ControlText (Jiang et al., 2025) validates distribution matching and establishes the Storia-AI classifier baseline, current methods fail in open-set generative environments. In order to resolve this limitation, we can test its metric-calibration framework against a ground truth built from an expert panel scoring in-the-wild generative crops. This establishes a unified verification protocol that has been missing from the natural-image, biological, and clean typographic literature.


2.7 Synthesis
Although these five areas of literature are mature, their capabilities remain fragmented and have not been combined into a single pipeline. Legacy methods localize layouts but assume topological font fidelity (Sheikh et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2025). Generative studies analyze the micro-warp deformations that violate this assumption, but they treat these distortions as bugs to suppress rather than distributions to read (Gillani et al., 2025; Kondo et al., 2024). Meanwhile, open-set research models rejection scores only for coarse, object-level shifts in natural images (Hofmann et al., 2024; Karunanayake et al., 2025). Representation models isolate fine-grained visual traits but avoid generative distortions (Chowdhury et al., 2025; Oquab et al., 2024). Finally, measurement benchmarks evaluate structural distances only on clean or general translation datasets (Jiang et al., 2025; Zhang et al., 2024). The main challenge lies in connecting these domains. To address this, this thesis integrates attention-based layout isolation with a deformation-robust, trait-localized embedding. This embedding is then scored using an open-set framework calibrated for typographic shifts, and validated against human consensus using a coupled visual distance and conformal prediction protocol. The proposed framework operates post hoc without generative-domain annotations over a bounded open-set palette, and forms the foundation of our approach.
