# Chapter 2 — Review of Related Literature

## 2.1 Introduction

This chapter reviews the literature that bounds the problem this thesis addresses and supplies the components from which its solution is assembled. The review is organized not by chronology or by disciplinary subfield but by the structure of the problem itself, in a deliberately problem-first sequence: it establishes what the established paradigm cannot do, characterizes the phenomenon that defeats it, and then assembles — component by component — the apparatus required to succeed where the paradigm fails. Five thematic clusters trace this arc.

The review opens with the paradigm this thesis is defined against (Section 2.2). Visual font recognition, from Wang et al.'s (2015) domain-defining DeepFont system through classical OCR and geometric layout analysis to the recent migration toward attention-based localization, is uniformly constructed on a closed-set, rigid-glyph assumption: that every input glyph is a topologically faithful instantiation of a designed typeface, and that recognition is the recovery of a clean underlying character from a noise channel that obscures but does not dissolve it. This assumption is load-bearing, and it is precisely the assumption that generative AI text violates.

Section 2.3 then characterizes the violation. Text-to-image diffusion models produce, as their default and dominant output, glyphs whose topology was never copied from a vector master but synthesized by a continuous latent process — glyphs exhibiting the micro-warping, variable kerning, stroke discontinuity, and cross-character attention smearing that this thesis terms *typographic hallucination*. The literature establishes this deformation as endemic (Liu et al., 2024), mechanistically explained (Gillani et al., 2025; Du et al., 2025), concentrated in the typographic long tail (Zhuang et al., 2025), and intrinsic to the continuous latent structure of diffusion models as a class (Kondo et al., 2024) — but it studies this deformation exclusively to *suppress* it inside the generator, never to recover typeface identity from an already-deformed output.

The three remaining clusters assemble the recognizer this thesis proposes for exactly that recovery task. Section 2.4 reviews the open-set rejection machinery — energy-based, logit-rescaling, and rank-based scores (Hofmann et al., 2024; Djurisic et al., 2024; Karunanayake et al., 2025; Lu et al., 2025) — that decides when a deformed crop falls outside the known font manifold and must be routed to an "unknown" verdict rather than forced onto a closed-set label. Section 2.5 reviews the representation layer — disentangled contrastive features over a frozen, register-corrected, prompt-steered self-supervised ViT (Wang et al., 2024; Oquab et al., 2024; Darcet et al., 2024; Chowdhury et al., 2025; Li, Li, et al., 2024) — over which that rejection score is computed. Section 2.6 reviews the measurement layer — deformation-robust structural distance and calibrated Top-K prediction sets (Zhang et al., 2024; Li et al., 2025; Shi et al., 2024; Ding et al., 2025; Jiang et al., 2025) — against which the pipeline's outputs are validated. Section 2.7 synthesizes the unoccupied intersection these five clusters jointly define, and Section 2.8 formalizes the conceptual framework that binds them into a single inference architecture.

## 2.2 Legacy Font Recognition, OCR/Layout & Closed-Set Vector Assumptions

### 2.2.1 Domain Anchor: DeepFont and the Closed-Set Clean-Glyph Paradigm

The trajectory of visual font recognition as a scientific discipline traces to a single landmark contribution: Wang, Yang, Jin, Shechtman, Aseem, and Huang's (2015) DeepFont system, the first large-scale deep convolutional framework purpose-built for identifying typefaces from photographic image inputs. DeepFont's methodological innovations were inseparable from the assumptions built into its design. Training proceeded on a synthetic corpus of clean, programmatically rendered specimens — each character generated directly from the corresponding font file, yielding samples of metrically uniform stroke width, precisely controlled inter-character spacing, and zero topological ambiguity. This synthetic data strategy was not a compromise born of label scarcity but a deliberate design choice: generating training images from font files guaranteed that the discriminative features the CNN would learn — the ratio of thick to thin strokes, the geometry of serifs, the curvature of round terminals, the proportional relationships between ascenders and x-heights — were uncontaminated by rendering artifacts. The model learned to read the typeface, not the noise.

Performance was evaluated under the Top-1 and Top-3 accuracy protocol, which remains the standard benchmark for font identification to the present day. Under this protocol, a recognition is correct at Top-1 if the highest-scored class matches the ground-truth font, and correct at Top-3 if the ground truth appears among the three highest-scored classes. DeepFont's demonstrated success under this protocol validated the central claim of the clean-glyph paradigm: that a convolutional network trained on pristine synthetic specimens could generalize to real-world photographic inputs — scanned typeset pages, cropped magazine headlines, photographed storefronts — with commercially useful accuracy.

The theoretical underpinning of this success is a signal decomposition that clean-glyph training enforces: the discriminative representation learned by the CNN separates the *typographic identity signal* (which font) from the *environmental degradation channel* (how it was photographed). Under this decomposition, the recognizer's task is well-defined and bounded — every input image is presumed to contain a glyph whose topology was fixed at render time, and the question the network answers is which of the known fonts produced the observed topology. This is a closed-set assumption in the strict sense: the hypothesis space is the set of fonts in the training corpus, and every input is presumed to belong to exactly one element of that set.

The implications of this assumption for the present thesis are precise. When a generative AI model renders text, it does not copy a glyph from a font file and degrade it through a noise channel. Instead, it constructs a pixel arrangement whose local structure approximates the intended character through a probabilistic denoising process that is inherently topology-agnostic. The resulting image may visually resemble a typeface — it may even pass casual visual inspection — but it does not share the parametric identity of any vector master. DeepFont's signal decomposition fails at the first step: there is no recoverable typographic identity signal to separate from the noise, because the noise and the signal co-emerge from the same generative process. The present thesis adopts DeepFont's Top-1 and Top-3 accuracy protocol as its performance framework precisely because that protocol is the field's established standard, but it re-targets the entire pipeline at the morphologically unstable, topology-uncertain regime that DeepFont's training methodology categorically excluded.

### 2.2.2 Classical OCR and Geometric Layout Analysis: The Rigid-Glyph Assumption in Practice

The assumptions encoded in DeepFont's synthetic training protocol are not idiosyncratic; they reproduce, in a deep learning context, the same structural presupposition that governs the entire classical pipeline for optical character recognition and document layout analysis. Classical OCR and layout analysis achieve their highest performance on text that is anatomically correct — text in which each character preserves the topological contracts of its letterform — and that success is the clearest possible illustration of how performance ceilings encode architectural assumptions.

The contemporary OCR literature provides a representative benchmark. Ponnuru et al. (2024) implement a Tesseract-based OCR pipeline with adaptive thresholding to extract and digitize handwritten and smudged prescription data — a corpus that exhibits substantial within-sample variability in ink density, letterform idiosyncrasy, paper texture, and scanning compression. The reported accuracy of 98% on standard document scanning defects is an instructive figure, and its diagnostic value lies not in its magnitude but in what it reveals about the *character* of the noise that the system handles. Every defect tested — ink spread, scanner noise, smudging, handwriting variance — perturbs the *rendering quality* of a character without dissolving the underlying letterform structure. The stroke skeleton persists through ink spread; the loop closure persists through compression; the descender trajectory persists through handwriting idiosyncrasy. Adaptive thresholding and morphological cleaning recover the character because the character's topological identity — the contracted set of spatial relationships that define a letter — is not destroyed by these noise processes, only obscured. The 98% ceiling is achievable because the problem is one of signal recovery, and the signal is structurally intact beneath the corruption.

At the level of document layout, the same presupposition manifests as a commitment to rule-based geometric analysis. The classical approach to text-region detection, documented in Bhunia et al. (2018), treats each character or word block as an object with predictable, stable boundaries: a convex hull, a bounding box, or a connected-component region grown from a seed point under the assumption that the glyph interior is locally coherent. Bounding-box estimation algorithms of this class work because intentional inscription — mechanical printing, typesetting, or skilled handwriting — respects the topological contracts that define legible letterforms: loops are closed, terminals are stable, strokes do not bifurcate randomly. The classical closed-boundary analysis is thus an accurate computational model of its target phenomenon, but the target phenomenon is a restricted class of inputs: those in which human or mechanical agency guarantees that the relationship between a glyph and its intended typeface is one of faithful instantiation.

Together, Tesseract-based OCR (Ponnuru et al., 2024) and closed-boundary layout analysis (Bhunia et al., 2018) define the prior art on text-in-image processing for anatomically correct glyphs. Their diagnostic utility as prior-art anchors for this thesis is precisely that their failure mode is structurally legible. Both systems implicitly compute the same function: given an input image, recover the clean underlying character by inverting the noise that obscures it. This function is well-posed when the clean character exists as an antecedent state — when there is a pristine glyph that the noise was applied to. It is undefined when the noise and the signal co-emerged from a probabilistic generation process, as in the diffusion model regime that produces the generative AI text crops this thesis analyzes. In that regime, there is no antecedent clean glyph to recover; the glyph that appears in the image is not a degraded copy of a pristine rendering but the primary output of a system with no privileged access to the intended typeface's geometry. The 98% accuracy ceiling, far from being a standard to aspire to, is the performance achievable on a categorically different problem — and the gap between that ceiling and the performance of identical systems on generative AI text is the quantitative expression of the architectural assumption this thesis is designed to relax.

### 2.2.3 The Attention-Based Localization Bridge: Migration From Fixed Receptive Fields

The recognition that fixed CNN receptive fields impose inappropriate spatial priors for irregular text layouts has driven a sustained migration toward attention-based alternatives. This migration is the architectural precondition for any downstream font-identification system that must operate on in-the-wild generative AI outputs, where text may appear at arbitrary scales, orientations, and densities, and where the spatial structure of the text region is not recoverable from a geometric prior.

Wang, Lv, Wu, Xie, Lu, Wang, Zhan, and Su (2025) illustrate the upstream half of this migration in the domain of table detection and tabular data extraction from scanned document images. Their TransTab framework integrates a ViT backbone with self-attention to parse layout structures whose spatial relationships span variable-width columns, irregular cell merges, and multi-row headers that cannot be described by a convolution kernel of any fixed size. The self-attention mechanism processes all spatial positions jointly, allowing the model to condition its representation of any one layout element on the global context of the surrounding structure — a capability that fixed-receptive-field CNNs can approximate only by stacking many layers at the cost of exponentially growing parameter counts. TransTab then routes the attention-derived layout representation into an EasyOCR sub-module for character digitization, establishing a modular pipeline in which text localization (a spatial organization problem) and character recognition (a perceptual identity problem) are handled by architecturally distinct components whose outputs compose cleanly.

The relevance of TransTab to this thesis is architectural rather than domain-specific. The argument that Wang, Lv, et al. (2025) make — that a ViT with self-attention can model spatial structure that rigid CNN receptive fields cannot — transfers directly to the glyph-crop isolation problem that precedes font identification. A generative AI output is not a document with conventional typesetting; it is an image in which text regions may occupy arbitrary spatial configurations, where character baselines may drift, kerning may be irregular, and the separation between text foreground and pictorial background may not respect the binarization thresholds that classical OCR pre-processing assumes. Attention-based feature extraction, reading layout relationships at multiple spatial scales simultaneously, is the architectural prerequisite for isolating individual glyph crops from such images without imposing the rigid geometric priors that classical layout analysis requires.

Sheikh, Shehzadi, Hashmi, Stricker, and Afzal (2024) extend this migration to the fully unsupervised regime with UnSupDLA, a document layout analysis framework that eliminates the need for labeled bounding-box annotations by deriving layout masks from frozen self-supervised DINO encoders. UnSupDLA employs a loss reduction technique — formalized as $L_{\text{drop}}$ — to expand the exploration of layout region boundaries beyond what a supervised model constrained to annotated regions would discover, and refines the resulting pseudo-masks across multiple iterations to converge on reliable structural boundaries without ever consulting a ground-truth annotation. The methodology is label-free by design, motivated by the practical observation that annotated layout datasets are expensive to produce and rapidly become stale as document formats evolve.

The implications for this thesis are twofold. First, UnSupDLA's use of frozen DINO features for text-region segmentation establishes a direct precedent for the frozen-ViT approach this thesis adopts at the font-identification stage — the same encoder family, the same self-supervised pre-training, applied one step upstream in the same overall pipeline. The architectural continuity between unsupervised layout isolation (UnSupDLA) and self-supervised feature embedding (DINOv2, Section 2.5) is not incidental; it reflects the principled commitment to representations that do not require per-sample annotation, a commitment justified by the no-label constraint governing in-the-wild generative AI crops. Second, the pseudo-mask refinement paradigm of UnSupDLA provides methodological license for iterative, self-supervised training strategies — the only available option when the input distribution consists of generative AI outputs for which no font ground-truth exists.

The transition traced by TransTab and UnSupDLA — from fixed-receptive-field CNN localization to attention-based, self-supervised layout isolation — is thus the field's own acknowledgment that rigid geometric priors are insufficient for the spatially irregular text environments that constitute the real-world deployment scenario of this thesis. Critically, that acknowledgment does not extend to the font-identity step: these papers localize text regions and recognize characters, but neither asks which typeface was intended when the localized text was rendered. The present thesis occupies exactly the gap this localization migration opens: it accepts the modern, attention-based localization apparatus as upstream infrastructure and asks, for the first time, whether font identity can be recovered from the glyph crops produced when that infrastructure is applied to generative AI outputs.

### 2.2.4 Deep Learning Taxonomy and the Synthetic-Degradation Rationale

The architectural and methodological choices of this thesis — the selection of a discriminative metric-learning framework, the use of synthetic data with algorithmic degradation, and the training strategy combining clean specimens with distortion-augmented variants — require a theoretical scaffolding that locates these decisions within the established landscape of deep learning practice. Two foundational surveys supply this scaffolding.

Sarker (2021) provides the comprehensive taxonomy that organizes deep learning architectures along three primary axes: discriminative models, which learn the mapping from input space to label space; generative models, which learn the joint or marginal distribution of the data; and hybrid architectures, which combine both objectives within a single framework. The font recognizer proposed by this thesis operates within the discriminative regime: its function is to map a hallucinated glyph crop to a probability distribution over a closed palette of known typefaces, with an additional channel for open-set rejection when the crop falls outside the palette boundary. Sarker's treatment of convolutional feature hierarchies, attention mechanisms, transfer learning, and hyperparameter optimization provides the authoritative terminological and conceptual framework within which the architectural decisions of this thesis — specifically, the choice to freeze a self-supervised backbone and train only a lightweight metric head — can be positioned as a principled point in the discriminative design space rather than an ad hoc engineering selection.

Plastropoulos and Tegos (2024) complement the taxonomy with an empirical and theoretical case for data augmentation as a representation-shaping mechanism. Reviewing machine learning and deep learning approaches for object detection, semantic segmentation, and human action recognition, Plastropoulos and Tegos demonstrate that combining classical image pre-processing — noise reduction, adaptive scaling, morphological operations — with deep hierarchical feature extraction compels the model to learn *continuous contextual boundaries* on high-variance inputs rather than fitting to the surface textures of clean training images. The key theoretical claim, which this thesis borrows directly, is that augmentation performs an implicit form of distributional coverage expansion: by presenting the model during training with inputs that span the range of geometrically plausible perturbations, the resulting decision boundaries are robust to distributional shifts that would otherwise move test inputs off the clean-data manifold.

For this thesis, the augmentation argument takes the following specific form. Clean Google Fonts specimens are programmatically subjected to elastic warping, Gaussian blur and noise, edge compression, and randomized inter-character tracking variation — perturbations calibrated to simulate the topology-disrupting deformations characteristic of diffusion model text generation. The theoretical justification, supplied by Plastropoulos and Tegos (2024), is that training on degradation-simulated data reshapes the metric space so that decision boundaries between font families pass through regions of feature space that are stable under topological perturbation — precisely the perturbation regime that generative AI outputs inhabit. The trained recognizer is then evaluated not on its own augmentation distribution but on genuinely deformed generative outputs, and the claim this training strategy makes is that the representation space induced by degradation-augmented training is shared with the representation space of real deformed glyphs. Whether that claim holds — and to what degree — is one of this thesis's primary empirical questions.

Both Sarker (2021) and Plastropoulos and Tegos (2024) share a limitation equally important to note: neither addresses the typographic domain, and neither examines the specific deformation regime produced by diffusion model text generation. Sarker's examples draw from image classification, natural language processing, and time-series prediction; Plastropoulos and Tegos focus on object detection and action recognition benchmarks. The borrowing is therefore theoretical rather than empirical — the taxonomy and augmentation principles are applied to a new domain under new degradation conditions, and the empirical question of whether they transfer is answered by the experiments of this thesis rather than resolved by the cited literature. That gap between general principles and specific typography application is itself a component of the research contribution this thesis claims.

**Research Gap in the Legacy Recognition Paradigm.** The seven sources reviewed in this section collectively define the closed-set, geometrically-rigid paradigm that constitutes the established baseline for font recognition and document text analysis. Wang et al.'s (2015) DeepFont established the domain's evaluation protocol — clean-glyph synthetic training, Top-1/Top-3 accuracy measurement, closed-set classification — that successive systems have adopted without renegotiating the underlying assumptions. Classical OCR achieves near-ceiling performance on anatomically correct text, revealing through that ceiling the load-bearing assumption it cannot relax: that the glyph to be recognized is a topologically faithful instantiation of a designed typeface (Ponnuru et al., 2024; Bhunia et al., 2018). The recent migration toward attention-based and self-supervised layout analysis removes geometric rigidity from the localization step without addressing the morphological assumptions embedded in the font-identity step (Wang, Lv, et al., 2025; Sheikh et al., 2024). The deep learning taxonomy and augmentation literature licenses the synthetic-degradation methodology but has not been applied to typographic recognition under generative AI deformation conditions (Sarker, 2021; Plastropoulos & Tegos, 2024). None of these works asks whether font identity can be recovered from a glyph whose topology was not copied from a vector master but generated by a probabilistic latent model — a glyph in which deformation is not a noise channel to be inverted but an irreducible property of the generative process. The sections that follow characterize how that generative process produces its characteristic deformations (Section 2.3), then assemble the open-set rejection, metric embedding, and validation infrastructure required to address it (Sections 2.4–2.6).

## 2.3 Generative AI Typographic Hallucinations & Diffusion Latent Mutations

### 2.3.1 The Endemic Character of Generative Typographic Failure

The central premise of this thesis — that generative AI systems produce text whose glyph structure is morphologically unreliable — requires empirical grounding before it can serve as the basis for a research program. The relevant literature supplies that grounding with unusual directness: the failure rate of uncorrected text-to-image systems is not a tail risk to be managed but the dominant outcome to be characterized.

The most precisely quantified account of this failure rate appears in Liu, Liang, Liang, Luo, Li, Huang, and Yuan's (2024) investigation of visual text rendering in Stable Diffusion XL (SDXL). Liu et al. diagnosed a fundamental architectural limitation in the base pipeline: the standard text encoder treats a character sequence as a semantic unit and lacks the sub-character spatial awareness — glyph alignment, individual character geometry, positional correspondence between unicode codepoints and their pixel representations — required to produce typographically accurate output. On a purpose-built design benchmark evaluating the fidelity of rendered text against its specification, an uncorrected SDXL pipeline achieved fewer than 20% of renderings classifiable as accurate. This figure is not a performance metric to be improved by conventional scaling; it is an architectural diagnosis. The text encoder does not model glyphs as spatial objects, and the denoising process that constructs the output image does not receive a representation that reliably distinguishes one character's geometry from an adjacent one. Glyph-ByT5 — Liu et al.'s corrective intervention, which fine-tuned the encoder on glyph-conditioned data — achieved rendering accuracy approaching approximately 90%, confirming that the failure is architectural rather than fundamental. For the purposes of this thesis, however, the remediated system and the remediated figure are not the relevant data. The relevant data is the uncorrected baseline: in the absence of specialized architectural correction, a generative AI image containing text is far more likely to contain a deformed glyph than a faithfully rendered one. Deformation is the default output state, not the exceptional failure, and this thesis operates precisely in the regime that Liu et al.'s correction targets but does not retroactively resolve — the regime of already-generated, uncorrected outputs whose typographic structure must be interpreted rather than corrected.

Li, Song, Cai, Xiong, Yuan, and Wang (2025) supply a distinct empirical entry point that compounds the architectural diagnosis. Where Liu et al. (2024) measured rendering accuracy from the generation side, Li, Song, et al. measured font *recognition* accuracy from the perception side — specifically, the performance of state-of-the-art vision-language models (VLMs) on a purpose-built font recognition benchmark. Their findings document a systematic failure that persists across model size and prompting strategy: VLMs are readily derailed by a Stroop-type semantic interference, in which the lexical content of the rendered word — its meaning, its syntactic associations, its contextual connotations — competes with and often overrides the model's reading of the visual typeface texture. A model shown the word *heavy* in a light, condensed typeface is drawn toward a font designation consistent with the word's semantic weight rather than with the observed letterform geometry. Neither few-shot exemplar provision nor chain-of-thought decomposition reliably suppresses this semantic interference, and Li, Song, et al.'s (2025) benchmark results demonstrate that off-the-shelf VLMs do not constitute a viable font-identification solution.

The diagnostic value of Li, Song, et al.'s (2025) result for this thesis is dual. The Stroop effect they document operates even on *clean* typographic inputs — the rendered text in their benchmark is legible and anatomically correct, free of the glyph deformations that characterize generative AI outputs. The implication is that semantic distraction and glyph-level deformation are independently sufficient to undermine general-purpose recognition: even without deformation, VLMs fail to read typeface; with deformation added, the task is further compounded. A font identifier designed for deformed generative outputs therefore requires a dedicated visual texture pipeline that is architecturally isolated from the semantic content of the rendered word — a requirement that general-purpose multimodal models, trained to process semantic and perceptual information jointly, are structurally unsuited to meet. The negative result of Li, Song, et al. (2025) closes off the shortest apparent path to a solution and motivates the specialized metric-learning architecture this thesis develops.

### 2.3.2 The Mechanism of Deformation: Cross-Character Attention and Multi-Instance Entanglement

Establishing that typographic hallucination is endemic requires characterizing *how* it arises mechanistically. The causal account responsible for the micro-warping, stroke discontinuities, and inter-character blending that define glyph deformation in diffusion model outputs has been localized, with progressively increasing precision, in the attention structure of the underlying generative model.

Gillani, Baig, Khan, Shah, Mujeeb, and Ali (2025) provide the most precise mechanistic account in their TextPixs framework. Their analysis identifies uncontrolled cross-character attention as the proximate cause of glyph artifacts: during the diffusion denoising process, the attention map assigned to a given character's latent tokens is not spatially confined to that character's bounding region but bleeds into the spatial footprints of adjacent characters. This attention leakage allows neighboring character representations to superimpose in the image space during iterative denoising, producing the visual signatures this thesis treats as the hallucination signature — micro-warping at character boundaries as adjacent letterforms pull each other's stroke trajectories off-axis, variable kerning as spacing is synthesized over conflated cross-character representations, and partial character merging when adjacent glyphs attract strong mutual attention weights across the denoising trajectory. Gillani et al.'s corrective intervention — a character-aware attention *segregation* loss that enforces spatial independence between adjacent character attention maps, augmented by OCR-guided fine-tuning to anchor the denoising trajectory to lexically valid letterform targets — reduces Character Error Rate from 0.21 to 0.08 and Word Error Rate from 0.25 to 0.15. These corrected figures represent improvements; the baseline figures are measurements of the uncorrected mechanism. What TextPixs contributes to this thesis is not the correction but the diagnosis: the mechanism that produces the micro-warping and variable kerning this thesis's pipeline must read is cross-character attention smearing, and it is intrinsic to the standard diffusion architecture's treatment of multi-character sequences.

Du, Xie, Chen, Wang, Jiang, Zhang, Yang, and Tai (2025) extend the mechanistic account from the single-instance to the multi-instance regime in their TextCrafter framework. They identify a failure mode that intensifies in complex visual scenes: when multiple independent text strings appear in the same image — a poster with headline, caption, and logo typeface; a product label with brand name and variant descriptors — the attention representations for each text instance do not remain spatially isolated from one another. Instead, the different strings' latent representations become entangled across the cross-attention layers of the generator, producing glyphs that blend typographic properties from co-present strings, merge characters across text regions, or lose spatial coherence altogether. Du et al. address this through token insulation and attention-gating mechanisms that enforce inter-instance independence at the representation level, and supply **CVTG-2K** — 2,000 prompts specifically engineered to stress the multi-instance text generation regime, spanning varied text positions, instance quantities, character-sequence lengths, and visual attribute combinations — as a controlled stressor catalogue.

CVTG-2K's contribution to this thesis is methodological as well as mechanistic. The stressor dimensions Du et al. (2025) identify — positional variability, density of co-present text instances, sequence length variation, attribute diversity — translate directly into parameters for the thesis's synthetic data pipeline. The elastic warping, tracking randomization, and kerning jitter that the pipeline applies to clean Google Fonts specimens are calibrated to cover the same deformation conditions that CVTG-2K exercises in the generative context: the full stressor menu rather than isolated noise regimes. Where Gillani et al. (2025) name the mechanism, Du et al. (2025) catalogue the conditions under which it is maximally activated, and that catalogue is an empirical map of the input distribution this thesis's training suite is designed to approximate.

### 2.3.3 Long-Tail Concentration and Diffusion Latent Mutation as Structural Properties

The mechanisms documented in Section 2.3.2 — cross-character attention smearing and multi-instance attention entanglement — are not uniformly distributed across the typographic input space. The distributional shape of glyph hallucination has been characterized in the recent literature, and its non-uniformity imposes direct constraints on the design of a deformation-robust font identifier.

Zhuang, Huang, Fu, Chen, Lei, and Mao (2025) examine the specific subpopulation of generative text where degradation is most severe: the long tail of typographic rendering, comprising rare glyphs, small-scale text, and characters outside the high-frequency core of standard training corpora. Their HDGlyph framework hierarchically decouples the synthesis of text from non-text content and introduces a two-stage refinement process — a linguistic glyph network that encodes character-level shape constraints, followed by a refinement stage that corrects deformations in the initial draft rendering — to address this subpopulation specifically. The performance gains reported by Zhuang et al. against baseline systems are concentrated in the long-tail regime: approximately 5% improvement on English text and approximately 12% improvement on Chinese text, with the larger Chinese gain reflecting the greater morphological complexity of CJK glyphs and their correspondingly deeper underrepresentation in standard diffusion model training corpora.

The implications of this distributional shape for this thesis are acute and somewhat counterintuitive. The long tail of typographic rendering corresponds, in the context of a localized Google Fonts palette, to the less common typeface categories: display faces with idiosyncratic stroke geometries, highly individuated script letterforms, and monospace faces with constrained structural variation. These are precisely the font categories that a closed-set recognizer trained on pristine specimens would, in principle, have the most discriminative power over — their distinctive visual features should make them *easier* to identify than the homogeneous geometric sans-serifs that populate the training distribution's center. But Zhuang et al.'s (2025) finding that deformation is worst in the long tail means that the fonts most visually distinctive in the pristine domain are also most severely hallucinated in generative outputs. A deformation-robust font identifier must therefore exercise the greatest epistemic caution precisely in the category-space where a clean-glyph-trained recognizer would be most confident — and must represent uncertainty over these categories with calibrated prediction sets rather than overconfident single-class assignments.

Kondo, Takezaki, Haraguchi, and Uchida (2024) approach the structural origin of typographic mutation from the generation side rather than the recognition side. Their investigation of font style interpolation via latent diffusion models demonstrates that diffusion models do not represent font styles as discrete catalogued entities but as positions in a continuous latent manifold. Interpolating between two known font styles in the latent space of a diffusion model produces intermediate glyphs whose visual properties — stroke contrast, serif geometry, x-height proportions, weight distribution — are genuine blends that correspond to no named typeface in any commercial or open-source library. The interpolated outputs are not corrupted versions of either source font but morphologically novel entities, produced by a generative process that operates on continuous latent coordinates rather than discrete typographic identities.

Kondo et al.'s (2024) contribution provides peer-reviewed confirmation, from the internal structure of the generative model, of the thesis's central empirical claim: that generative AI text outputs are not perturbed copies of canonical typefaces but morphologically distinct entities produced by a continuous latent process that has no privileged access to the discrete identity categories of a font catalog. The latent interpolation Kondo et al. study as a design *feature* — generating aesthetically attractive intermediate font styles for typographic applications — is the same continuous manifold process that produces typographic hallucination as a recognition *problem*. When a text-to-image model renders a word in a "serif" style, it samples from a latent region associated with serif characteristics without consulting a font file; the output glyph's visual proximity to any named typeface is probabilistic and approximate, not definitional. This is the precise sense in which the present thesis's font identifier must operate on continuous typographic distance metrics rather than categorical identity matching: the target domain is a continuous distribution over glyph space, not a finite set of discrete typeface instances.

### 2.3.4 Evaluation Taxonomy: A Unified Framework for Measuring Generative Typography

The literature reviewed in Sections 2.3.1–2.3.3 produces a dispersed array of evaluation instruments: rendering accuracy at the pipeline level (Liu et al., 2024), semantic-interference failure rates for perception-side models (Li, Song, et al., 2025), Character Error Rate and Word Error Rate as degradation measures (Gillani et al., 2025), scene-complexity stressor categories (Du et al., 2025), and rendering gain margins differentiated by typographic subpopulation (Zhuang et al., 2025). These measures are complementary but disconnected — each paper introduces its own evaluation protocol in service of its own corrective objective, without providing a common coordinate system within which they could be interpreted jointly. Shu, Zeng, Zhao, Chen, Li, Yang, Zhou, Rota, Bai, Jin, Yin, and Sebe (2025) supply the cross-cutting evaluation taxonomy that situates these dispersed metrics within a single measurement framework.

The VTPBench and VTPScore contributions of Shu et al. (2025) emerge from a comprehensive review of visual text processing that spans generation, editing, and recognition tasks across more than 20 evaluated models. VTPBench consolidates multiple evaluation datasets into a unified multi-task benchmark that enables cross-model and cross-task comparison at a scale not previously attempted in the literature. VTPScore introduces a quality metric grounded in multimodal large language model (MLLM) assessment that distills legibility, positional accuracy, and stylistic consistency into a single interpretable score, without requiring the per-character annotation overhead that character-level metrics such as CER and WER demand. Together, these instruments provide the evaluation infrastructure that the growing visual text generation literature had lacked: a common reference framework against which individual papers' task-specific results can be positioned.

The structural contribution of Shu et al. (2025) to this thesis is taxonomic rather than methodological. VTPBench and VTPScore provide the coordinate system within which the evaluation instruments of the upstream literature — rendering accuracy, CER/WER, the CVTG-2K stressor categories, long-tail rendering gain — can be cited as complementary rather than competing measures, each addressing a distinct dimension of typographic quality under a shared evaluation umbrella. When this thesis subsequently introduces its own evaluation protocol — typographic distance via structural similarity and calibrated Top-K font prediction — the VTPBench/VTPScore taxonomy licenses the claim that these measures address dimensions of typographic quality that the existing evaluation infrastructure does not cover. VTPScore evaluates whether the *right characters* appear in a *readable form*; this thesis's protocol asks whether the *intended typeface identity* survives the generative process sufficiently to be recovered. The gap between those two measurement objectives — correctness of characters versus recoverability of typeface — is precisely the measurement gap the thesis's own protocols are designed to fill.

**Research Gap in the Generative Hallucination Literature.** The seven sources reviewed in this section establish, with increasing precision, that typographic hallucination in generative AI outputs is endemic (Liu et al., 2024; Li, Song, et al., 2025), mechanistically characterized at the level of cross-character attention dynamics (Gillani et al., 2025; Du et al., 2025), distributionally concentrated in the typographic long tail (Zhuang et al., 2025), intrinsic to the continuous latent structure of diffusion models as a class (Kondo et al., 2024), and measurable within a growing unified evaluation taxonomy (Shu et al., 2025). What unites every source in this section, however, is a shared objective that is precisely the inverse of this thesis's goal: each paper studies typographic deformation in order to *suppress* it inside the generator. The corrective interventions — glyph-conditioned text encoders (Liu et al., 2024), character-aware attention segregation losses (Gillani et al., 2025), token insulation and attention gating (Du et al., 2025), hierarchical decoupling and two-stage refinement (Zhuang et al., 2025) — are architectural modifications to the generative pipeline, evaluated on whether the *output of the corrected system* is less deformed than the output of the uncorrected baseline. None of this literature asks whether the output of an *uncorrected* pipeline — a deformed glyph already emitted — can be used to recover information about the typeface the generating prompt intended. The evaluation metrics that accompany these corrections — CER, WER, VTPScore — are uniformly OCR-centric: they measure whether the correct *characters* appear, not whether the typographic *identity* of the intended typeface has survived the generative process in a recoverable form. The inversion this thesis performs — treating the documented deformation distribution not as a defect to eliminate before the input reaches a font identifier, but as the *input distribution* of the font identifier itself — is the unoccupied intersection between this generative hallucination literature and the open-set recognition, metric embedding, and structural distance literature reviewed in Sections 2.4–2.6.

## 2.4 Logit-Space Thresholds & Open-Set Rejection Scores

The two preceding sections establish complementary halves of the problem this thesis addresses: the closed-set paradigm underlying classical font recognition is architecturally incapable of handling inputs outside its training distribution (Section 2.2), and generative AI pipelines produce, as their default output, exactly the morphologically unstable glyphs such paradigms cannot represent (Section 2.3). The present section addresses the inferential machinery required to convert that architectural gap into a functioning rejection decision — specifically, how a trained classifier, confronted with an input whose visual structure places it outside the manifold of its training data, can detect that fact and route the input to an "unknown" bin rather than forcing a closed-set label onto it. The literature reviewed here organizes this detection problem into a consistent two-component framework: a *rejection score*, computed as a scalar function of the model's internal representations, and a *threshold*, against which that score is compared to route the input either toward a recognized class or toward explicit rejection. The central question this section addresses is where in the network the most informative score lives, and how recent methodological advances have sharpened the theoretical basis for locating it there.

### 2.4.1 Energy-Score Foundations and Logit-Space Rescaling

The two most influential post-hoc architectures for computing out-of-distribution rejection scores — energy-based detection and logit rescaling — share an underlying intuition: that the model's output layer, which in a standard classifier produces a probability distribution over known classes, encodes more information about the input's relationship to the training distribution than the raw probability values alone reveal. The disagreement between these approaches concerns which transformation of the output layer extracts that information most reliably.

Hofmann, Schmid, Lehner, Klotz, and Hochreiter (2024) ground their detection framework in the energy formalism of modern Hopfield networks. In this formalism, the Hopfield energy assigned to an input is a scalar quantity derived from the model's stored patterns and the current input's alignment with them: inputs that lie near the dense manifold of training-distribution examples are assigned low energy by the network's retrieval dynamics, while inputs that lie in the low-density regions separating training clusters — the regions occupied by out-of-distribution examples — are assigned high energy. The energy score operationalizes the geometric intuition that known inputs cluster near training-data centers while unknown inputs occupy the sparse interstitial spaces between them. Crucially, Hofmann et al.'s implementation — which they term Energy-based Hopfield Boosting (EHB) — augments standard energy detection with an outlier-exposure training scheme that mines *boundary-adjacent* auxiliary outliers: artificial training examples positioned deliberately near the ID/OOD boundary, close enough to the known distribution that naive energy detectors would misclassify them, but definitionally outside it. Training on these hard boundary examples resharpens the energy gradient at the manifold boundary, making the rejection decision most discriminative precisely where the score would otherwise be least certain.

The empirical record of Hofmann et al.'s (2024) EHB framework demonstrates the practical value of this boundary-sharpening strategy. On the benchmark task of outlier-exposure OOD detection, EHB achieves a False Positive Rate at 95% True Positive Rate (FPR95) of 0.92% on CIFAR-10, 7.94% on CIFAR-100, and 36.60% on ImageNet-1K — state-of-the-art results representing substantial reductions from the corresponding baselines of 2.28%, 11.76%, and 50.74%. The improvement from 50.74% to 36.60% on ImageNet-1K — a 1,000-class benchmark with substantial within-class visual diversity — is particularly instructive because it demonstrates that boundary-adjacent outlier mining remains effective in high-dimensional, multiclass classification regimes of the same order of magnitude as a localized font palette. The FPR95 metric itself has direct operational relevance to this thesis: it measures the fraction of genuinely OOD crops that would be passed as recognized fonts at a threshold calibrated to recover 95% of true in-palette examples — precisely the tradeoff a production font identifier must navigate.

The conceptual contribution of Hofmann et al. (2024) to this thesis is not architectural but structural. The energy formalism supplies a principled language for the central rejection question: is a deformed glyph crop on-manifold with respect to the known font distribution, or does it occupy the low-density interstitial space that the model's training data does not cover? A crop warped by a diffusion model's continuous latent process may visually approximate multiple known fonts without cleanly falling within any of their training-data clusters — it may share stroke-weight characteristics with one family, terminal geometry with another, and x-height proportions with a third. The energy score is designed exactly to detect this kind of manifold-boundary ambiguity. However, the critical limitation — unresolved by Hofmann et al. and inherited by any application of EHB to typography — is that the energy boundary evaluated on natural-image OOD benchmarks separates categorically different object types: textures versus natural objects, one scene category versus another. Whether the energy boundary separating, say, the Roboto manifold from the Lato manifold in font-feature space is sufficiently sharp to support reliable rejection at the level of typographic granularity — where the discriminative visual distance between related sans-serif families may be smaller than the within-class variation between CIFAR-10's automobile instances — is an empirical question the EHB literature does not address.

Djurisic, Liu, and Nikolic's (2024) Logit Scaling for OOD Detection (LTS) addresses the score-location question by a different route. Rather than operating on the Hopfield energy of the final layer, LTS operates directly on the logit vector — the pre-softmax activations the classification head produces for each known class. The motivating observation is that the magnitude of the maximum logit, while correlated with model confidence, does not fully capture the discriminative information available in the logit vector about whether the input is in-distribution. Different inputs produce logit vectors at different global scales: some inputs drive all logit activations to large magnitudes, reflecting high certainty about the class, while others produce small-magnitude activations across the board, reflecting generic low-confidence responses. LTS addresses this scale variability by deriving a sample-specific normalization scalar from the input's feature vector and applying it to rescale the entire logit vector before applying the rejection score. The effect of this rescaling is to make the scoring function invariant to global magnitude variation — which confounds naive maximum-logit detectors — while preserving the relative structure of the logit vector, specifically the pattern of which classes score high versus low, which is the structure that carries the ID/OOD signal.

The practical significance of LTS is its unconditional training-data freedom. The normalization scalar is derived entirely from the feature representation of the test input itself, without consulting any auxiliary outlier samples, without retraining the classifier, and without requiring any modification to the network architecture. Djurisic et al. (2024) validate LTS across CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100, ImageNet, and the OpenOOD benchmark, demonstrating consistent improvements over unscaled logit-based detectors while maintaining strict architectural agnosticism — the same normalization procedure applies to ResNet, ViT, and other backbone families without modification. For a thesis operating under the constraint that the font classifier must generalize to a new domain without access to font-domain OOD training examples, LTS's post-hoc, architecture-agnostic character is operationally attractive. The open question, which the typography domain surfaces for the first time, is whether the feature-derived normalization scalar of LTS captures the right variation in a typographic deployment — where the within-class variation of a single font family under elastic deformation may produce logit-scale variability that mimics the between-class variation of different fonts, potentially causing the normalization to suppress signal rather than extract it.

Taken together, Hofmann et al. (2024) and Djurisic et al. (2024) define the principal axes of the post-hoc OOD detection literature: energy-based scores that respond to manifold geometry, and logit-space scores that respond to the magnitude and structure of classification activations. Both operate after training is complete, both are deployable without architecture modification, and both convert a closed-set classifier's output into an open-set binary decision by applying a scalar threshold. The shared limitation, from this thesis's perspective, is that neither has been evaluated in a domain where the in-distribution and out-of-distribution classes differ only in terms of subtle typographic geometry — where the visual distance between a known Garamond and a hallucinated serif-adjacent glyph is potentially smaller than the visual distance between CIFAR-10's automobile and truck categories. Section 2.4.2 addresses this granularity problem by attending not just to the top of the logit vector but to the informative structure of what comes after it.

### 2.4.2 Rank-Based Rejection and the Survey Scaffold: ExCeL and the Open-Set Literature

The energy-based and logit-rescaling approaches in Section 2.4.1 both operate on scalar summaries of the output layer — the Hopfield energy or the magnitude-normalized maximum logit — and compare that scalar to a threshold. A distinct and theoretically complementary line of work argues that the full *ordinal structure* of the logit vector, not just its maximum element, contains systematically exploitable information about whether an input is in-distribution. The most direct development of this insight in the current OOD literature is Karunanayake, Seneviratne, and Chawla's (2025) ExCeL framework — a contribution whose implications extend beyond its published evaluation benchmarks to provide the conceptual foundation for this thesis's approach to Top-K font prediction as an anomaly signal rather than merely a soft accuracy measure.

Karunanayake et al.'s (2025) theoretical argument begins with an observation about the differential structure of logit vectors on in-distribution versus out-of-distribution inputs that maximum-logit detectors underexploit. On inputs drawn from the training distribution, the ranking of classes by logit score is highly *deterministic*: not only is the top-ranked class reliably correct, but the *order* in which the remaining classes rank — second, third, fourth, fifth — is reproducible across examples from the same class. This determinism is not incidental; it reflects the learned geometry of the representation space. The feature neighborhoods of closely related training classes overlap in predictable ways, so that examples of class A consistently activate class B's logit more strongly than class C's, because A and B share more feature-space proximity than A and C. In the typographic context, this translates to a stable "disambiguation sequence": Old Style serifs consistently rank other Old Style serifs as their nearest competitors; geometric sans-serifs surface other geometric sans-serifs as runner-ups; display typefaces with idiosyncratic stroke geometries find their nearest neighbors among other display faces. The runner-up ranking is not noise — it encodes the typographic adjacency structure of the feature space.

On out-of-distribution inputs, this ordinal determinism breaks down in a characteristic way. An input whose visual structure does not coherently belong to any training class lacks stable alignment with any class's feature cluster. The secondary logit rankings become noisy, inconsistent, and atypical of any pattern established during training: the runner-up classes span diverse typographic families with no structural connection, and the logit gaps between them do not reflect any coherent proximity relationship. This breakdown is the open-set signature that ExCeL is designed to detect. Karunanayake et al.'s framework fuses two information sources: the *extreme* signal of the maximum logit, which maximum-logit detectors already use, and the *collective* signal of how the runner-up logit distribution compares to the typical runner-up patterns observed during training for the predicted class. The combined score is more discriminative than either source alone because it captures both "does the model predict with high confidence?" and "does the uncertainty pattern look like in-distribution uncertainty or random noise?" — the former being detectable by existing methods, the latter being the incremental contribution of the ranking signal.

Evaluated on CIFAR-100 and ImageNet-200, ExCeL achieves top-5 performance across both near-OOD and far-OOD conditions. The near-OOD category — inputs that are visually similar to training data but from different classes — is the theoretically harder case and the one most directly analogous to the typographic OOD regime this thesis addresses: a hallucinated glyph that resembles a known font but was generated by a continuous latent process without copying its geometry is precisely the near-OOD scenario, visually proximate to training data but structurally distinguishable at the feature level. ExCeL's demonstrated effectiveness in this near-OOD regime is the strongest available evidence in the natural-image literature that rank-based signals are informative for the kind of subtle distributional shift that typographic deformation introduces — though direct empirical validation in the typography domain remains absent.

The mapping from ExCeL's logit-ranking framework to this thesis's Top-K font identification protocol is both conceptually exact and practically productive. In the font classification context, the *extreme* information in ExCeL's formulation corresponds to the top-1 predicted font — the highest-scoring candidate in the closed-set prediction, the output that existing commercial font identifiers treat as the complete answer. The *collective* information corresponds to the ordering and spacing of the runner-up font candidates at positions K=2 through K=5 of the softmax distribution. When a hallucinated glyph crop is presented to the classifier, an in-distribution crop — one whose deformation has preserved sufficient typographic structure to place it recognizably within a known font's feature neighborhood — will produce a runner-up ranking pattern consistent with that font's training neighbors: the fonts with the most similar visual geometry will rank predictably second and third, and the logit gaps between positions will reflect the known typographic adjacency structure of the palette. A Garamond-deformed crop, for example, should produce a runner-up sequence dominated by other Old Style serifs — EB Garamond, Cormorant Garamond, Libre Baskerville, Playfair Display — with logit gaps reflecting the fine-grained proximity differences among those families.

An out-of-distribution crop — one whose deformation has carried it into an interstitial region between palette fonts, or outside the palette boundary entirely — will produce a runner-up pattern inconsistent with any training font's typical disambiguation sequence: the runner-up candidates will span dissimilar typographic families with no coherent structural relationship, the logit gaps will not reflect any adjacency pattern established during training, and the global ordinal structure will be atypical of any in-distribution example. This insight transforms the Top-K font shortlist from a passive accuracy metric into an active anomaly indicator. The runner-up ranking pattern serves as a second-order anomaly signal that is richer than the top-1 logit alone and sensitive to precisely the typographic cross-family incoherence that characterizes a genuinely out-of-palette hallucination. ExCeL provides the theoretical justification and empirical proof of concept for this signal in the natural-image domain; this thesis extends and tests it in the typography domain for the first time.

Lu, Wang, Sheng, He, Zheng, and Liang's (2025) task-oriented survey of recent advances in out-of-distribution detection supplies the structural scaffolding that situates both the energy/logit methods of Section 2.4.1 and ExCeL's rank-based approach within the broader OOD detection landscape. Lu et al. organize the literature into three primary deployment categories: training-driven methods, which modify the training objective or dataset composition to make internal representations more discriminative between in- and out-of-distribution examples; training-agnostic (post-hoc) methods, which apply detection functions to a pre-trained model's outputs without modifying training; and large pretrained model methods, which adapt foundation models — ViTs, CLIP-family architectures — to open-set detection tasks. Each of the methods reviewed in this section — EHB (Hofmann et al., 2024), LTS (Djurisic et al., 2024), and ExCeL (Karunanayake et al., 2025) — falls within the training-agnostic category.

This categorical placement is operationally decisive for this thesis. A font classifier deployed on generative AI outputs cannot, in practice, be retrained on a balanced mix of in-palette and out-of-palette typographic examples, because the out-of-palette category is by definition the class of all possible hallucinated glyphs that do not correspond to any of the 50–100 Google Fonts in the target palette — an infinite, unbounded class that cannot be enumerated, sampled, or labeled in advance. Any viable open-set rejection scheme must operate post-hoc on a pre-trained classifier, deriving its rejection signal from the classifier's output structure without requiring the OOD class to be characterized prior to deployment. Lu et al.'s (2025) survey confirms that the energy and logit-ranking scores reviewed in this section are methodologically aligned with this post-hoc constraint, and — critically — that this constraint is not an engineering limitation but a principled design choice consistent with the state of practice in production OOD detection systems. The survey's treatment of test-time adaptation further clarifies that calibrating a rejection threshold on a held-out validation set of in-palette crops, without exposure to OOD examples, is a standard and validated deployment strategy, licensing this thesis's threshold calibration protocol.

Lu et al.'s (2025) additional coverage of multimodal OOD settings — in which visual and semantic signals may conflict, as they do in the Stroop-effect scenario documented by Li, Song, et al. (2025) for font recognition — reinforces the case for a visual-feature-only pipeline isolated from the semantic content of the rendered word. The survey's finding that post-hoc logit-space and energy-space detectors achieve competitive performance across modalities and backbone architectures without requiring domain-specific training directly licenses this thesis's approach: freeze a pre-trained font classifier's backbone, apply a post-hoc ExCeL-style scoring function to the logit vector it produces for each glyph crop, use the runner-up ranking pattern as a secondary anomaly indicator, and route crops with atypical ordinal patterns to the open-set output without requiring labeled OOD training data from the typography domain.

**Research Gap in Open-Set Rejection.** The four sources reviewed in this section supply a mature and theoretically grounded framework for open-set rejection decisions: energy-based manifold scores that respond to the geometry of training clusters (Hofmann et al., 2024), architecture-agnostic logit rescaling that normalizes for scale variability (Djurisic et al., 2024), runner-up-ranking signals that detect the collapse of ordinal determinism in out-of-distribution inputs (Karunanayake et al., 2025), and a task-oriented survey that situates all three within the validated post-hoc deployment paradigm (Lu et al., 2025). The gap is precisely specified: every evaluation benchmark in this section consists of natural-image OOD tasks — CIFAR-10/100, ImageNet-1K/200, and the OpenOOD suite — in which in-distribution and out-of-distribution classes differ in object-level semantic content, texture category, or scene structure. None of these methods has been evaluated on *typographic* OOD: the task of detecting whether a glyph crop generated by a diffusion model operating on a continuous latent manifold falls within the feature-space manifold of known typefaces in a localized font palette, or instead occupies the interstitial region produced by micro-warping, variable kerning, and cross-character attention smearing. Whether the energy gradient at a font-family boundary is steep enough to support reliable rejection thresholds, whether logit-magnitude normalization captures the deformation-induced scale variability that typographic OOD introduces, and whether runner-up ranking collapses in a measurable and consistent manner for genuinely hallucinated glyphs — these are open empirical questions that constitute the central experimental contribution of this thesis's open-set detection component, addressed through the metric-learning embedding reviewed in Section 2.5 and the structural validation protocols of Section 2.6.

## 2.5 Feature Disentanglement & Frozen-ViT Metric Embedding

The open-set rejection machinery reviewed in Section 2.4 — energy-based manifold scoring, logit-magnitude rescaling, and runner-up-ranking anomaly detection — constitutes the *decision layer* of this thesis's pipeline. But every decision made by that layer is only as reliable as the geometric structure of the embedding space the scores are computed in. If the feature vectors representing a Garamond specimen and a Lato specimen are not separated by a sufficiently steep energy gradient, the EHB manifold score will fail to distinguish them at typographic granularity; if the logit-magnitude normalization introduced by LTS conflates the within-class deformation variance of a single font with the between-class variance separating different font families, the normalization will suppress rather than extract the open-set signal; and if the runner-up logit rankings exploited by ExCeL are not ordinal-stable for in-palette crops, the collapse of that stability for hallucinated crops provides no discriminative anomaly cue. The reliability of the Section 2.4 rejection framework is, in this sense, a downstream function of the quality of the underlying representation: the rejection score selects inputs from the high-energy, low-confidence, ordinal-unstable region of the embedding space, but the geometry of that space — which regions are high-energy, which activations are magnitudinally confounded, which rankings are or are not ordinal-stable — is determined at the representation level. The present section reviews the literature that governs that representation level: the theoretical and empirical grounding for constructing a feature embedding that is simultaneously *disentangled* (axis-aligned and sparse, so that typographic identity dimensions do not entangle with deformation artifacts), *frozen* (derived from a self-supervised backbone pre-trained on broad visual experience and not modified for the font domain), and *trait-localized* (steered by lightweight prompting mechanisms to attend to the structural discriminators of typeface identity rather than the lexical content of the rendered word).

### 2.5.1 Non-Negative Contrastive Features and the Frozen Self-Supervised Backbone

The first precondition for a reliable open-set embedding in the typographic domain is axis-alignment: the dimensions of the feature space that encode typographic identity — serif versus sans-serif contrast, stroke-weight distribution, terminal geometry, x-height proportions — must not be systematically entangled with the dimensions that encode glyph deformation artifacts — elastic warp amplitude, boundary blurring, tracking irregularity. If these dimensions are entangled, as they commonly are in unconstrained contrastive embeddings, a deformed crop of a sans-serif typeface may be represented at a feature-space location that more closely resembles a clean crop of a visually adjacent serif family than a deformed crop of the same sans-serif, because the deformation artifact dimensions have distorted the typographic identity dimensions in a direction that crosses a family boundary. The rejection score then faces an ill-posed problem: the energy gradient at the family boundary conflates genuine open-set inputs with in-distribution crops whose deformation has shifted them across the boundary in feature space.

Wang, Zhang, Guo, and Wang (2024) address this entanglement problem at its representational root through their Non-negative Contrastive Learning (NCL) framework. The central theoretical contribution of NCL is a non-negativity constraint imposed on the learned contrastive feature vectors — an adaptation of non-negative matrix factorization (NMF) principles to the contrastive learning objective. The motivation derives from the identifiability result associated with NMF-type constraints: non-negative factors are uniquely recoverable from observations under mild independence assumptions, and the resulting basis vectors tend to correspond to interpretable constituent properties of the input rather than distributed, overlapping representations that mix multiple underlying attributes. In the contrastive learning context, imposing non-negativity on the learned embeddings yields a "renaissance" of NMF's representational benefits — sparse, axis-aligned features in which each dimension corresponds to a recognizable visual attribute — while retaining the discriminative power of contrastive objectives trained on large corpora. Wang et al. (2024) demonstrate that NCL outperforms standard contrastive learning on feature disentanglement benchmarks, feature selection tasks, and downstream linear classification, with theoretical guarantees on identifiability and generalization that standard contrastive embeddings lack.

The application of NCL's axis-alignment property to the typographic domain is structurally motivated rather than merely conceptual. When feature dimensions are sparse and interpretable, the axes that encode typographic identity — operationalized as differential activation strengths for serif geometry, stroke-contrast ratio, letterform proportional system, and counter-space curvature — are separated in the feature space from the axes that encode deformation state. A deformed serif crop then maintains its high activation on the serif-identity axes while accumulating activation on the deformation-artifact axes without those two activations interfering with each other. The energy boundary between the serif and sans-serif manifolds remains geometrically stable under deformation, because the deformation dimensions are orthogonal to the typographic-identity dimensions rather than systematically correlated with them. This orthogonality is the formal precondition for the energy-based and logit-rescaling rejection scores of Section 2.4 to function at typographic granularity: a steeper, cleaner energy gradient between known font families is achievable only when the embedding geometry does not allow deformation artifacts to masquerade as typographic family transitions. Wang et al. (2024) supply the theoretical basis and empirical proof of concept for this property on natural-image disentanglement benchmarks; the transferability of the same non-negativity constraint to the typographic domain — where the "parts" that NMF-style constraints would recover are typographic sub-features rather than natural object parts — constitutes an empirical question this thesis is positioned to address.

The self-supervised backbone on which the NCL metric head operates requires independent justification. Oquab, Darcet, Moutakanni, Vo, Szafraniec, and colleagues (2024) present DINOv2 as a self-supervised Vision Transformer trained by a distillation-based procedure that requires no image labels of any kind. DINOv2's training on a large curated corpus of diverse visual images instills features that transfer to fine-grained visual classification, semantic segmentation, instance retrieval, and depth estimation — tasks spanning the full range from global scene semantics to fine-grained local feature discrimination — without any domain-specific fine-tuning. The critical property that makes DINOv2 the preferred backbone for this thesis is not its performance ceiling under full fine-tuning but its quality under complete freezing: linear-probe evaluations — which assess whether a simple linear classifier applied to frozen features can solve a fine-grained discrimination task without modifying the backbone — demonstrate competitive fine-grained classification performance, implying that the discrimination required to separate fine-grained visual categories is already encoded in the frozen feature space and is accessible to a linear function of those features. For this thesis, the implication is that a lightweight NCL metric head trained on top of frozen DINOv2 patch embeddings can access typographic discriminative information without any encoder modification — the expensive and label-hungry step of fine-tuning a large backbone on font-domain data is bypassed in favor of learning only the metric alignment of a pre-existing, already-discriminative feature space.

DINOv2's emergent spatial properties further strengthen the case for this thesis's architectural choice. Oquab et al. (2024) demonstrate that DINOv2's attention maps exhibit unsupervised part grouping: individual attention heads, without explicit part supervision, learn to attend to semantically coherent spatial regions of input images — foreground objects, structural boundaries, texture regions — producing attention maps that closely resemble human-annotated segment boundaries. In the glyph-crop context, this emergent part grouping translates to the capacity to attend to the anatomical regions of a typeface — the stroke terminals, the bowl junctions, the counter-space geometry — that distinguish one font family from another, without requiring those regions to be manually annotated during training. The discriminative information this thesis requires is spatial and local (specific anatomical features of letterforms) rather than global (the overall gestalt of the word image), and DINOv2's patch-level feature extraction, which produces an independent feature vector for each spatial position in the image, is architecturally suited to reading this local structural information regardless of the overall word length or spatial composition of the glyph crop.

The foundational backbone configuration requires one additional correction before it is suitable for glyph-crop feature extraction. Darcet, Oquab, Mairal, and Bojanowski (2024) document a systematic pathology in self-supervised Vision Transformers, including DINOv2, whose consequences are particularly acute in the glyph-crop regime. A subset of tokens in the ViT's input sequence exhibit anomalously high activation norms and are spatially localized to low-information regions of the input image — background areas, spatially uniform zones, featureless padding. Darcet et al. (2024) demonstrate that these "artifact tokens" are not noise but functional elements: the model repurposes them as global information storage registers in the absence of explicitly designated computational slots. The consequence for downstream feature quality is substantial. Artifact tokens receive disproportionate cross-attention weights from other tokens in the sequence, corrupting the spatial structure of the resulting attention maps and introducing systematic bias in the dense feature representations that downstream tasks consume. In fine-grained dense prediction tasks — semantic segmentation, depth estimation, instance segmentation — corrupted features produce characteristic artifacts at artifact-token locations. Darcet et al.'s remedy is the addition of dedicated *register* tokens to the ViT input sequence: a small number of explicitly designated learnable tokens that serve as global information storage locations, so that the model no longer needs to hijack feature tokens for this purpose. With register tokens added, artifact-free attention maps emerge and dense feature quality improves substantially across downstream evaluations.

For this thesis, the register-token finding is not a background technical concern but a direct prerequisite for reliable glyph feature extraction. A typographic crop is precisely the category of input for which the artifact-token pathology is most likely to be severe: the discriminative signal is concentrated in a small spatial area — the letterform's strokes, counters, and terminals — while the surrounding image region is an uninformative, often uniform background. This sparse-signal geometry is exactly the low-information-region condition that drives self-supervised ViTs to generate artifact tokens. If artifact tokens accumulate in the plain background surrounding the glyph and receive disproportionate cross-attention weights, they corrupt the patch features that encode stroke geometry — producing feature maps in which the discriminative typographic information is masked by artifact-induced noise. The register-token mechanism of Darcet et al. (2024) addresses this degradation at its structural source, ensuring that the DINOv2 backbone produces clean, artifact-free patch features for thin-stroke glyph crops on plain grounds. The combination of DINOv2's frozen fine-grained feature quality (Oquab et al., 2024) and register-token artifact correction (Darcet et al., 2024) constitutes the foundational backbone condition on which the NCL-based disentanglement objective (Wang et al., 2024) and the lightweight trait-localization mechanisms of Section 2.5.2 operate. Together, these three contributions define the *representation layer* that provides the embedding geometry required for the Section 2.4 rejection scores to function at typographic granularity.

### 2.5.2 Trait Localization, Label-Free Distillation, and Metric-Topology Foundations

A clean, disentangled, artifact-free feature space is necessary but not sufficient for typographic discrimination at the granularity this thesis requires. Fine-grained visual recognition tasks — distinguishing species of birds, makes of automobiles, varieties of aircraft — have established that even high-quality self-supervised features may represent multiple visual dimensions simultaneously, only some of which are discriminatively relevant for the target task. A general-purpose backbone trained on diverse visual data encodes foreground object identity, background scene statistics, texture, spatial composition, and semantic content jointly; the features it produces for a glyph crop will encode not only the typographic structure of the letterform but also the semantic content of the word being rendered — exactly the Stroop-type semantic interference that Li, Song, Cai, Xiong, Yuan, and Wang (2025) document as the primary failure mode of vision-language models in font recognition (Section 2.3). Directing the frozen backbone's attention to the specific discriminative patches that encode typographic identity, rather than the semantic content of the rendered word, requires a targeted steering mechanism that does not modify the backbone through full fine-tuning.

Chowdhury, Mehrab, Campolongo, Rubenstein, Stewart, Karpatne, Berger-Wolf, Su, and Chao (2025) develop exactly such a mechanism in their Prompt-CAM framework. Operating on a pre-trained, frozen ViT, Prompt-CAM learns class-specific input prompts — small learnable token sequences prepended to the ViT input — such that the true-class prompt must attend specifically to the spatial patches that are uniquely discriminative for that class, rather than to patches shared across many classes or irrelevant to class identity. The mechanism exploits the structure of ViT cross-attention: a class-specific prompt token that must predict the correct class label is incentivized, through gradient-based prompt learning, to assign high attention weights to the spatial locations that distinguish its class from all visual alternatives. Prompt-CAM validates this mechanism across twelve fine-grained visual recognition domains spanning birds, fish, insects, cars, and several additional categories, consistently producing attention maps that correspond to domain-relevant discriminative parts: wing patterning in bird recognition, fin geometry in fish recognition, decorative marking distribution in insect recognition.

The translation of Prompt-CAM's mechanism to the typographic domain is precise and structurally motivated. A class-specific prompt for a typeface such as Garamond, learned on frozen DINOv2 features, would attend to the spatial patches encoding the features uniquely discriminative for Garamond within a fine-grained font palette: the characteristic Old Style bracketed serifs, the moderate optical stroke contrast, the specific proportional ratio of capital height to x-height. Crucially, the same prompt ignores the semantic content of the rendered word — whether it is "Typography," "Design," "Sample," or any other lexical sequence — because the word's semantic identity is not uniquely discriminative for Garamond relative to other typefaces in the palette. Prompt-CAM does not prevent the backbone from encoding semantic content in its feature representations; it merely ensures that the class-discriminative attention path routes through the typographic-structure patches rather than the semantic-content patches. This is the architectural mechanism that addresses Stroop-type interference at the attention level, prior to the logit space where Li, Song, et al. (2025) observe its effects on off-the-shelf vision-language models. The class-specific prompts are lightweight relative to the backbone — they add a small number of learnable parameters per class and require no modification of the frozen encoder — making Prompt-CAM's integration into this thesis's pipeline computationally feasible without the full fine-tuning cost that conventional domain adaptation would require.

The layout-independence of the Prompt-CAM mechanism further strengthens its relevance to generative AI text crops. Class-specific prompts that attend to local typographic patches — stroke terminals, bowl junctions, counter geometry — read these features from wherever they appear in the spatial grid of the glyph crop, regardless of which word is rendered, how many characters it contains, or what kerning and spacing variations the diffusion process has introduced between them. The relevant patch features are present at the locations occupied by the letterform structure, and the prompt's attention weights respond to those features at those locations without requiring a fixed spatial template. This layout-independence is what makes frozen-ViT prompt-based localization architecturally superior, for this thesis's application, to convolutional approaches that apply fixed-size receptive fields to character images: a prompt attention mechanism reads discriminative typographic structure from wherever the strokes and terminals happen to fall in the image, accommodating the spatial irregularity that diffusion-generated glyph crops exhibit.

The final component of the embedding framework addresses the label constraint that governs adaptation to in-the-wild generative AI crops. The pipeline requires that the metric head — the NCL-constrained contrastive objective and the Prompt-CAM class-specific prompts — be adapted to a localized palette of Google Fonts without per-glyph font-identity labels from the generative AI domain. No dataset of generative AI text crops paired with verified ground-truth typeface labels currently exists; annotation of such data at the scale required for training would demand either a controlled generation pipeline that preserves exact typeface specifications — which does not reflect the uncorrected deployment scenario — or expert human annotation at a scale inconsistent with this thesis's data strategy. Li, Li, Fu, Zhang, Wang, Chen, and Yang (2024) address an equivalent constraint in the vision-language domain through their PromptKD framework, which distills a large teacher CLIP model into a compact student model via learnable visual prompts, without requiring image-level label supervision for the student's training domain. PromptKD aligns the student's output distribution to the teacher's by minimizing the Kullback-Leibler divergence between their logit vectors over *unlabeled* domain images. The teacher's class-text embeddings — pre-computed vector representations of the class label strings — are shared with the student and held fixed; the student learns only the visual prompt that maps its frozen visual encoder's features to the teacher's logit space, without consulting per-image ground-truth labels at any point during training.

The principle PromptKD establishes — that label-free alignment of a student to a teacher's logit distribution over unlabeled domain images suffices to transfer discriminative capability across a domain shift — applies directly to this thesis's distillation scenario. A teacher font classifier trained on clean Google Fonts specimens provides the reference logit distribution; the student, receiving as input unlabeled generative AI glyph crops that exhibit the elastic warping, stroke discontinuities, and tracking irregularity documented in Section 2.3, learns visual prompts that align its logit output to the teacher's distribution for the corresponding class, training the metric head to map deformed crops to the same region of logit space as their clean counterparts. The unlabeled student training images are the generative AI crops themselves — abundant, easily obtainable from any text-to-image generation pipeline, and representative of the actual deployment distribution without requiring annotation. Li, Li, et al. (2024) validate PromptKD on domain adaptation benchmarks where the student's training images differ systematically from the teacher's in rendering style, viewpoint, or photographic conditions, demonstrating that KL-divergence logit alignment over unlabeled data is sufficient to transfer discriminative capability across distribution shifts of that scale. The gap between clean Google Fonts render specimens (the teacher's training distribution) and generative AI glyph crops (the student's adaptation distribution) constitutes precisely such a domain shift, and the PromptKD distillation mechanism, properly adapted to the typographic domain, provides the label-free pathway through which this thesis's metric head acquires sensitivity to deformed inputs without per-glyph annotation.

Underlying all three mechanisms reviewed in this section — NCL's axis-aligned disentanglement, Prompt-CAM's class-specific attention steering, and PromptKD's label-free logit-distribution alignment — is a deeper theoretical claim about the appropriate mathematical structure for representing deformed, out-of-distribution inputs: that the mapping from input space to class assignments should be understood not as a categorical function from inputs to discrete labels but as a continuous function from inputs to *proximity rankings* within a structured metric space. Research within the deep metric learning and feature topology literature (Huang & Zhou, 2022) formalizes this claim by investigating methods through which deep networks decompose complex, high-variance target domains into structured feature vectors that encode inter-instance distance relationships rather than absolute class memberships. The foundational insight is that a font crop deformed by a generative process to a degree that places it off the clean training manifold of any single known font is not, for that reason, uninformative: it still occupies a position in the metric space relative to all known fonts, and that position encodes soft proximity information — how close it is to Garamond relative to Crimson, relative to EB Garamond, relative to Libre Baskerville — that a categorical prediction discards by forcing a hard assignment to whichever font's manifold boundary the crop happens to cross. The metric-topology formalism supports instead a *continuous proximity ranking* — a softmax distribution over palette classes whose ordinal structure encodes the relative typographic distances between the deformed input and each known font — and provides the theoretical scaffolding for treating that distribution as the primary inference output rather than an intermediate artifact to be argmaxed away. This continuous-ranking perspective is the representational foundation on which this thesis's Top-K accuracy protocol, typographic distance metrics (Section 2.6), and ExCeL-style runner-up ranking anomaly detection (Section 2.4) all converge: each of these components presupposes that the embedding's continuous proximity structure is the carrier of discriminative information, and that collapsing it to a single categorical prediction discards the typographic adjacency signal that is the most informative available indicator of hallucination severity.

**Research Gap in the Representation Layer.** The six sources reviewed in this section constitute the representational foundation of this thesis's metric-learning pipeline — supplying disentangled contrastive features that separate typographic identity from deformation artifacts (Wang et al., 2024), a frozen all-purpose ViT backbone with demonstrated fine-grained linear-probe performance (Oquab et al., 2024), a register-token mechanism that eliminates artifact-token corruption in the sparse-signal glyph-crop regime (Darcet et al., 2024), class-specific visual prompts that direct frozen ViT attention to typographic discriminative features rather than lexical content (Chowdhury et al., 2025), label-free prompt distillation that transfers discriminative capability from a clean-glyph teacher to an unlabeled deformed-crop student without per-glyph annotation (Li, Li, et al., 2024), and a metric-topology theoretical framework that licenses continuous proximity ranking over known fonts as the appropriate representation for a deformed, out-of-distribution glyph's feature-space position (Huang & Zhou, 2022). The gap that none of these sources addresses is empirical rather than theoretical: every validation context in this section is either a natural-image fine-grained benchmark (CUB-200, Stanford Cars, IP102, and analogous datasets) or a *clean* typographic benchmark — the linear-probe evaluations of DINOv2 operate on undeformed classification images, and the font domain assessments referenced in the PromptKD adaptation literature operate on typographically well-formed text specimens rather than probabilistically hallucinated crops. No study in this section presents results for the deformed typographic regime: glyphs whose topology has been probabilistically altered by a diffusion model's continuous latent process, exhibiting elastic warping, stroke discontinuities, variable kerning, and the cross-character attention smearing documented in Section 2.3. The open empirical questions — whether frozen DINOv2 features remain separable at the typographic family boundary under the deformation amplitudes characteristic of uncorrected generative outputs; whether Prompt-CAM's class-specific prompts continue to localize to discriminative typographic patches when those patches are partially disrupted by warp and blur; whether the label-free PromptKD distillation produces a metric head that maps deformed crops to the teacher's distribution regions with sufficient fidelity to support ExCeL-style ordinal-stability anomaly detection — are precisely the empirical questions that constitute the primary contribution of this thesis's metric embedding experiments and that distinguish the present work from all prior applications of frozen-ViT metric learning to visual fine-grained recognition.

## 2.6 Advanced Visual Distance Metrics (SSIM) & Human-Proxy Ground-Truth Alignment

The four preceding sections of this chapter have assembled three of the four components required for the thesis's inference pipeline: a characterization of the deformation distribution the identifier must read (Section 2.3), the open-set rejection score machinery that routes an out-of-palette crop to an "unknown" bin rather than forcing a closed-set label (Section 2.4), and the disentangled, trait-localized, continuously ranked embedding over which that score computes (Section 2.5). What remains is the *measurement layer* — the protocols by which the quality of the system's outputs is assessed and by which the pipeline's claims are evaluated against an empirical standard. The design of this measurement layer is not ancillary but constitutive: the choice of typographic distance metric determines what "closeness" to the correct font means in the morphologically irregular glyph space this thesis occupies; the choice of prediction-set calibration mechanism determines whether a Top-K shortlist of candidate typefaces constitutes a statistically grounded uncertainty claim or merely a ranked heuristic; and the choice of ground-truth establishment methodology determines the reference against which both the distance and the calibration are ultimately judged. The present section reviews the literature that grounds these three choices — deformation-robust structural distance (Section 2.6.1) and calibrated Top-K prediction sets over an open-set font palette with human-proxy alignment (Section 2.6.2) — and argues that the unified measurement protocol this thesis advances is precisely the intersection that the existing literature has prepared but not yet occupied.

### 2.6.1 Deformation-Robust Structural Distance: Beyond Pixel-Aligned Evaluation

A measurement architecture for deformation-robust font identification faces a foundational challenge that standard image quality metrics are not designed to meet. The classical Structural Similarity Index Measure (SSIM), introduced by Wang, Bovik, Sheikh, and Simoncelli (2004), assesses perceptual image quality by comparing the luminance, contrast, and structural organization of two images at corresponding pixel coordinates. This pixel-correspondence requirement is a structural assumption built into the SSIM formulation: the metric assigns maximum structural similarity when the spatial arrangement of brightness patterns in the test image matches the spatial arrangement in the reference image at the same pixel location. For standard image quality assessment — evaluating the effects of compression artifacts, sensor noise, or resolution reduction on a reference — this assumption is appropriate, because the degraded image is a perturbed copy of the reference whose spatial content is registered to it. The assumption collapses, however, under exactly the conditions that characterize the comparison this thesis's evaluation protocol requires: measuring the structural similarity between a clean, re-rendered font specimen and a hallucinated glyph crop whose topology has been probabilistically deformed by a diffusion model's continuous latent process. The elastic warping, stroke-boundary blurring, and variable kerning that deformation introduces shift the spatial locations of typographic features — the serif terminals, the counter-space boundaries, the bowl junctions — away from their positions in the clean reference. Classical SSIM, computing structure at corresponding pixel coordinates, penalizes these spatial shifts as dissimilarity even when the underlying typographic identity is intact, producing unreliably low similarity scores for structurally related pairs and making it impossible to distinguish a deformed crop of the correct font from a clean crop of an entirely different font family.

Zhang, Chen, Zhao, and Wang (2024) directly address this limitation in their DeepSSIM framework, which reconstitutes structural similarity measurement in deep feature space rather than pixel space. DeepSSIM computes SSIM not over raw image intensity maps but over the feature activations that a deep neural network assigns to each image, reasoning that feature representations produced by a trained network are invariant to precise spatial registration of visual elements while remaining sensitive to their structural identity. The foundational insight is that a feature extractor trained on large visual corpora encodes structural properties — the co-occurrence of high-frequency boundary signals, the topology of connected regions, the orientation energy of local edge configurations — at a level of abstraction not anchored to specific pixel coordinates. A serif terminal represented in the feature space of a deep encoder is identified by its local structural properties rather than by its position, so that an elastically warped version of the same terminal, displaced in pixel space, still occupies a nearby region in feature space. DeepSSIM augments this feature-space similarity computation with an attention calibration step that corrects for systematic attention deviation between the reference and test feature maps — a correction that addresses the residual positional offsets that deep features do not fully abstract away under global warping or spatial retargeting operations, ensuring that the feature-space SSIM computation is not contaminated by these offsets. Zhang et al. (2024) situate DeepSSIM explicitly in the geometrically-misaligned evaluation regime — the scenario where reference and test images are not pixel-registered — and demonstrate that the combined deep-feature-plus-attention-calibration approach maintains reliable similarity scores precisely in the regime where classical SSIM collapses. The co-authorship of Zhou Wang — the originator of the original SSIM metric — on DeepSSIM provides direct theoretical lineage from the classical structural-similarity literature to the present adaptation of it.

For this thesis, DeepSSIM operationalizes the *typographic distance* component of the evaluation protocol in the following specific form. After the metric pipeline produces a predicted font from the Google Fonts palette for a given hallucinated glyph crop, the predicted font is used to re-render the same word — or the same character set detected in the crop — at the appropriate size and composition, producing a clean reference specimen in the predicted typeface. This clean re-render and the original hallucinated crop are then passed through the DeepSSIM pipeline, which computes their structural similarity in deep feature space with attention calibration to correct for the geometric misalignment between the pristine re-render and the warped original. The resulting score quantifies, on a continuous scale, how much structural evidence the hallucinated crop shares with its predicted font's canonical representation — a measure that, unlike pixel-level SSIM, is resistant to the spatial displacements introduced by glyph warping, and unlike character-level metrics such as CER or WER (Gillani et al., 2025), specifically captures *typographic structure* rather than character identity. The measurement closes the loop between the prediction (which font?) and the evaluation (how much does the crop structurally resemble that font, given the crop is deformed?), answering both questions within the same structural-similarity framework without requiring pixel-level registration between the hallucinated crop and the clean reference.

Li, Chen, Wang, Ma, Bovik, and Zhang (2025) supply a complementary and methodologically independent grounding for structure-based similarity evaluation in their SAMScore framework. Rather than operating on the activations of a classification-trained deep network, SAMScore constructs its structural similarity measurement over the embeddings produced by the Segment Anything Model (SAM) — a foundation model for image segmentation trained to identify and describe coherent spatial regions across a broad range of visual inputs. SAMScore's premise is that structural correspondence between a source and a transformed image is most accurately measured by comparing their *content structure* — the topological organization of semantically coherent regions — rather than by comparing either pixel intensities or classification-task-specific deep features. A structurally correct transformation preserves the content regions of the source image — their shapes, spatial relationships, boundary configurations — even when it alters their appearance, and SAM's embeddings, trained to represent spatial region structure rather than semantic class identity, are naturally positioned to capture this preservation. Li, Chen, et al. (2025) validate SAMScore as the best-performing structural metric across 19 image translation tasks, demonstrating superior correlation with human structural quality judgments compared to both pixel-space SSIM and classification-feature-based perceptual metrics. The co-authorship of Alan Conrad Bovik — a founding contributor to the SSIM literature alongside Zhou Wang — situates SAMScore within the same structural image quality assessment tradition that DeepSSIM extends, providing a second independent derivation of deformation-robust structural distance from within the same intellectual lineage.

The typographic implication of SAMScore is distinct from but complementary to DeepSSIM's. DeepSSIM addresses geometric misalignment between structurally related pairs by abstracting away pixel-level registration requirements; SAMScore addresses the case where the *content structure* of a typographic image — the spatial topology of the glyph's strokes, loops, and counters as discrete spatial regions — must be compared between a deformed crop and a clean re-render without relying on any assumption about the spatial alignment of corresponding regions. In a glyph crop where elastic deformation has redistributed the topological relationship between, for example, the bowl of a lowercase /b/ and its adjacent vertical stroke, SAM's region-structure embeddings capture the *structural fact* that a bowl exists, a stroke exists, and they are connected, independently of the specific pixel coordinates at which each is located. SAMScore thus provides a region-topology based structural distance complementary to DeepSSIM's boundary-and-texture-based feature similarity, and the two metrics, applied jointly to the re-render-and-compare evaluation pipeline, triangulate typographic structural similarity from two independent representational perspectives — neither of which requires pixel-level registration between the hallucinated crop and the clean reference.

The theoretical foundation for applying structural distance metrics to highly distorted and geometrically irregular inputs — including the deformed typographic distributions this thesis examines — is further grounded in the soft computing and structural optimization literature. Umer et al. (2022), in an Applied Soft Computing contribution examining robust classification optimization frameworks for high-variance visual arrays, establish the principled justification for treating structural distance as a continuous function over unknown or geometrically disrupted input geometries, rather than confining its computation to the pixel-registered or topologically-intact regimes where classical SSIM was developed. The formalization developed by Umer et al. (2022) supports the use of continuous probability spreads and softmax-weighted structural distance computations — the precise combination this thesis employs in its typographic distance protocol — by demonstrating that these tools produce reliable structural ordering even in high-variance, distortion-heavy visual regimes where discrete similarity labels or pixel-level matching functions lose validity. This theoretical scaffolding licenses the inferential step from "this DeepSSIM or SAMScore value was obtained for a deformed-to-clean pair" to "this value can be meaningfully compared across pairs and used to rank predictions by typographic proximity" — a step that would require additional justification absent a formal treatment of structural distance optimization under high distortion.

The re-render-and-compare protocol combining DeepSSIM (Zhang et al., 2024), SAMScore (Li et al., 2025), and the structural-distance optimization framework (Umer et al., 2022) constitutes this thesis's *typographic distance* measure: a composite structural similarity score assessing how much structural evidence a hallucinated glyph crop shares with the clean reference specimen of its predicted font, robust to the spatial displacements and topological modifications that diffusion model generation introduces. The score is continuous — it yields a real-valued proximity rather than a binary correct/incorrect verdict — and it maps naturally onto the continuous proximity structure that the metric-learning embedding (Section 2.5) targets: a high typographic distance score for the Top-1 predicted font is evidence that the metric embedding has correctly captured the crop's structural proximity to that font; a low score despite a confident Top-1 prediction is evidence of a qualitative mismatch — the logit-space signal may have overclaimed the prediction's reliability, and the typographic distance provides an independent, post-prediction structural verification of the embedding's output.

### 2.6.2 Calibrated Top-K Prediction Sets, Font-Fidelity Distribution Matching, and Human-Proxy Ground-Truth Alignment

The typographic distance metric of Section 2.6.1 measures *how close* a specific prediction is to the correct answer, but it does not quantify *how confident* the system should be in that prediction — how many candidate fonts must be included in a shortlist to provide a statistically warranted claim that the true typeface is among them. A naive fixed-K shortlist that always returns the three highest-scoring candidates treats all predictions with equal confidence, regardless of the metric embedding's certainty about the input crop's feature-space position. This uniform approach conflates two qualitatively distinct situations: a glyph crop whose deformation has landed it near the center of a single font's feature-space cluster — where the Top-1 prediction is already highly reliable and a Top-3 shortlist is conservatively broad — and a glyph crop whose deformation has carried it into the interstitial region between two or three font families, where no single prediction is reliable and the true font may not appear in a three-element shortlist at all. The distinction has practical consequences for any downstream pipeline consuming the system's output: that pipeline requires not merely a ranked list but a *reliable* ranked list — one whose coverage guarantee ("the true font is among these K candidates") holds at a specified confidence level across the full distribution of glyph inputs, including the long-tail cases where deformation is worst.

Shi, Ghosh, Belkhouja, Doppa, and Yan (2024) formalize exactly this requirement through their RC3P framework — Rank-Calibrated Class-Conditional Conformal Prediction. RC3P addresses the prediction-set construction problem in multi-class classification: given a trained classifier's soft probability outputs for a test input, construct the smallest set of classes that is guaranteed, at a specified confidence level such as 90% or 95%, to contain the true class. Standard conformal prediction achieves marginal coverage — the guarantee holds on average across all test inputs — but fails to provide class-conditional coverage, where each individual class's prediction sets independently meet the specified guarantee. This failure is particularly acute for imbalanced class distributions, where high-frequency classes' prediction sets are over-large and low-frequency classes' prediction sets are under-reliable. RC3P addresses both problems through *label-rank calibration*: it augments conformal prediction thresholds with a correction derived from the distribution of true-class rank positions in the training softmax distribution, tightening thresholds only for classes where the top-k error is small enough to support tighter bounds, and adapting coverage separately for each class based on the classifier's observed difficulty on that class during calibration. Shi et al. (2024) demonstrate that RC3P reduces average prediction set size by approximately 26.25% relative to standard class-conditional conformal prediction while maintaining class-conditional coverage guarantees — producing shortlists that contain the true class at the specified level for each class individually, not merely on average.

The transfer of RC3P's calibrated prediction-set framework to this thesis's font identification protocol is direct and principled. The font classifier's softmax output for a given glyph crop is a probability distribution over the palette's typefaces; applying RC3P's rank-calibrated threshold transforms this distribution into a *prediction set* — a set of candidate fonts that provably contains the true font at the specified coverage level, as calibrated on a held-out validation set of clean Google Fonts specimens. The prediction set replaces the fixed-K Top-K shortlist with a *coverage-guaranteed Top-K* whose cardinality varies by input: a crop near the center of a known font's cluster requires a small set (perhaps K=1 or K=2) to achieve 90% coverage, while a crop in an interstitial region between families requires a larger set (perhaps K=4 or K=5) to meet the same guarantee. This variable-set coverage is both statistically principled — each shortlist emitted by the system is accompanied by a formal confidence claim rather than a heuristic count — and practically informative: a downstream reconstruction pipeline can adapt its behavior to the prediction set's cardinality, presenting a single high-confidence candidate when available and a broader shortlist when the crop's deformation leaves the true font genuinely ambiguous.

Ding, Fermanian, and Salmon (2025) extend the conformal prediction framework to the specific sub-distribution that this thesis's long-tail analysis (Section 2.3) identifies as the hardest case: rare and underrepresented classes in a highly imbalanced classification problem. Their contribution addresses a known failure mode of standard conformal prediction in the long-tail regime: marginal coverage guarantees achieved by holding total prediction-set size constant tend to sacrifice class-conditional coverage for rare classes, for which calibrated thresholds produce prediction sets smaller than warranted by the classifier's actual uncertainty on those inputs. Ding et al.'s approach introduces a prevalence-adjusted softmax score that reweights the distribution according to each class's frequency in the training data, compensating for the systematic underconfidence of softmax probabilities on rare classes, and an interpolation mechanism between marginal and class-conditional thresholds that allows the practitioner to trade per-class coverage guarantee tightness against prediction-set size. Validated on Pl@ntNet-300K and iNaturalist — natural history datasets with 1,000 to 8,000 highly imbalanced classes whose distributional structure mirrors a font palette in which common humanist sans-serifs vastly outnumber rare display or script typefaces — Ding et al.'s (2025) prevalence-adjusted conformal method maintains reliable per-class coverage guarantees for long-tail classes that standard calibration would misrepresent.

The typographic relevance of Ding et al.'s (2025) contribution maps directly onto the long-tail structure of the Google Fonts palette in a way that extends Zhuang et al.'s (2025) finding about deformation severity. Section 2.3 established that generative deformation is worst for rare and visually complex glyph types — the long tail of the rendering distribution, where the diffusion model's training data is sparsest and cross-character attention smearing most severe. Ding et al.'s (2025) prevalence-adjusted conformal calibration ensures that the prediction set covers rare, display, and script typefaces in the palette with the same specified coverage guarantee as common geometric and humanist sans-serifs, even though the metric classifier's training distribution is dominated by the latter. The combined application of RC3P (Shi et al., 2024) for base class-conditional calibration and the prevalence-adjusted long-tail extension (Ding et al., 2025) produces a prediction-set protocol that provides reliable coverage guarantees across the full typographic range — from the most common humanist sans-serifs to the rarest ornamental display faces — precisely the range that this thesis's claim of deformation-robustness over a *localized* palette must substantiate.

A typography-native precedent for evaluating font-identity predictions by comparing prediction distributions — rather than simply scoring binary top-K accuracy — is provided by Jiang, Yuan, Bai, Hao, Yin, Hu, Liao, Ungar, and Taylor (2025) in their ControlText framework. ControlText confronts the open problem of font evaluation in the absence of exact ground-truth labels: in complex, multi-instance text rendering scenarios where many fonts look visually similar ("fuzzy fonts"), binary correct/incorrect judgment at the font-name level is an unreliable evaluation criterion. Jiang et al.'s solution is to measure font fidelity by comparing the *top-k probability distributions* that a pre-trained font classifier assigns to an input glyph and to a generated image: if the classifier places similar probability mass on similar font classes for both the reference and generated images, the generated text is considered to have faithfully reproduced the intended font style, regardless of whether the maximum-probability class is identical. They formalize this comparison through two metrics — **L2@k**, which measures the Euclidean distance between the top-k classifier probability vectors of the reference and generated images, and **cos@k**, which measures the cosine similarity between the same vectors — evaluated using the Storia-AI Google Font Classifier, a pre-trained classifier over 3,474 Google Fonts, as the reference embedding. The ControlText framework establishes L2@k and cos@k as principled measures of typographic fidelity for cases where the font ground-truth is ambiguous, approximate, or unavailable.

The contribution of ControlText (Jiang et al., 2025) to this thesis is double. First, L2@k and cos@k constitute a typography-domain precedent for exactly the Top-K distribution-matching approach this thesis's evaluation extends: measuring not whether the predicted font matches a hard label but whether the Top-K probability *structure* of the prediction distribution matches the reference distribution, capturing typographic similarity at the level of the embedding's continuous metric space rather than at the level of discrete class assignments. Second — and of particular methodological significance — the Storia-AI Google Font Classifier that Jiang et al. (2025) employ as the reference embedding for L2@k/cos@k computation is identical to the Storia-AI `font-classify/` baseline this thesis adopts as its own reference classifier. The appearance of the same classifier in an independent, contemporaneous publication evaluating font-fidelity in generative typography is a convergent validation: the field's independent assessment confirms that the Storia-AI classifier constitutes the current standard reference embedding for font identity in the generative text evaluation literature, and that using it as the comparison standard for both this thesis's metric-learning pipeline evaluation and its fidelity distance computation is consistent with the state of the art. The L2@k/cos@k protocol thus serves this thesis both as a measurement template — compare the Top-K distribution of the metric pipeline's predictions against the distribution the baseline classifier produces on clean specimens — and as external confirmation that the measurement framework is methodologically appropriate for the domain.

The calibrated prediction sets of Shi et al. (2024) and Ding et al. (2025) and the distribution-matching fidelity metrics of Jiang et al. (2025) together constitute the *uncertainty quantification and fidelity* component of this thesis's evaluation protocol. They answer the question "how trustworthy is the Top-K shortlist?" by transforming the raw softmax output of the font classifier into a statistically calibrated set with class-conditional coverage guarantees, verifying that the set's distribution structure matches the reference classifier's distribution on clean specimens, and differentiating between inputs where a small set is warranted by high classifier confidence and inputs where a larger set is required by genuine typographic ambiguity.

The ground truth against which these calibrated prediction sets and distribution-matching scores are ultimately judged in this thesis's empirical evaluation is established by this study's own human-proxy methodology. The present research employs a three-person expert consensus panel evaluating a sample of [N] generative AI image text crops drawn from the deployment distribution, with each assessor independently assigning a ground-truth typeface category — or an "unknown / hallucinated" verdict for crops whose visual structure does not correspond to any identifiable palette font — under a structured evaluation protocol. Consensus across the panel, operationalized as agreement among at least two of the three assessors, determines the ground-truth label against which the system's calibrated prediction set is evaluated. This ground-truth establishment procedure constitutes an original methodological contribution of the present study rather than a replication of any prior literature; it is grounded in the conformal prediction calibration framework (Shi et al., 2024; Ding et al., 2025) by adopting the class-conditional coverage criterion as the evaluation target — the prediction set is considered to have provided correct coverage for a given input if and only if the expert-consensus ground-truth label is among the set's elements — and by adapting the prevalence-adjusted calibration logic to the long-tail sub-distribution that the panel identifies as the highest-ambiguity region of the palette. The human-panel methodology thus serves two simultaneous roles: it provides the evaluation standard against which the statistical coverage guarantees derived from RC3P and its long-tail extension are verified on the actual deployment distribution, and it produces an independent, human-perceptual estimate of the prevalence and severity of genuine typographic ambiguity in uncorrected generative AI outputs — the empirical quantity no prior evaluation framework has measured for in-the-wild generative text crops assessed specifically for font-identity recoverability.

**Research Gap in the Measurement Infrastructure.** The six sources reviewed in this section establish the measurement infrastructure this thesis's evaluation protocol requires but do not, individually or collectively, occupy the specific measurement position this thesis claims. On the structural distance side, DeepSSIM (Zhang et al., 2024) and SAMScore (Li et al., 2025) supply deformation-robust structural similarity measures validated on natural-image quality assessment and image translation benchmarks — never on the specific comparison between a clean font re-render and a diffusion-model-hallucinated glyph crop; the structural-distance optimization framework (Umer et al., 2022) supplies the theoretical justification for applying continuous distance measures to high-variance, distorted geometries but does not instantiate the re-render-and-compare typographic evaluation pipeline this thesis defines. On the uncertainty side, RC3P (Shi et al., 2024) and the long-tail conformal extension (Ding et al., 2025) supply class-conditional prediction sets with coverage guarantees validated on natural-image and biological object classification — never on a font palette under the deformation conditions documented in Section 2.3; ControlText's L2@k/cos@k metrics (Jiang et al., 2025) supply a typography-native distribution-matching fidelity measure validated on clean or controlledly rendered glyph inputs, not on the probabilistically deformed outputs of uncorrected text-to-image generation pipelines. No existing work couples a deformation-robust structural distance measure — one that survives the geometric misalignment between a hallucinated crop and a clean re-render — with a calibrated Top-K font prediction set over a localized open-set Google Fonts palette, and no prior evaluation protocol assesses this coupling against a human-expert consensus ground truth established specifically for in-the-wild generative AI text crops. The unified validation protocol this thesis advances — typographic distance (DeepSSIM/SAMScore-based re-render-and-compare) combined with calibrated Top-K (RC3P augmented by prevalence-adjusted long-tail coverage), distribution-matching fidelity (L2@k/cos@k over the Storia-AI baseline embedding), and human-panel consensus ground truth — is the measurement contribution of the present work, closing the gap from the structural distance literature through the conformal calibration literature to the typography-native distribution evaluation literature, and grounding all three against an empirical human-judgment standard purpose-built for the hallucinated-glyph deployment scenario that every prior evaluation framework addressed from a categorically different input distribution.

## 2.7 Synthesis and Research Gap

The five thematic clusters reviewed in this chapter are, individually, mature bodies of work, each internally complete on its own terms. Read together, however, they describe a set of capabilities that has never been assembled into a single pipeline directed at a single problem — and the gap this thesis occupies is defined precisely by the seams between them.

The legacy paradigm (Section 2.2) supplies an evaluation protocol and a closed-set architecture that presuppose topological fidelity; its own recent evolution toward attention-based, self-supervised localization (Wang, Lv, et al., 2025; Sheikh et al., 2024) removes geometric rigidity from the *localization* step while leaving the morphological assumptions of the *font-identity* step entirely intact. No work in this cluster asks whether font identity survives when the glyph was generated rather than instantiated. The generative-hallucination literature (Section 2.3) characterizes, with increasing mechanistic precision, exactly the deformation distribution that violates those assumptions — but every source studies deformation to eliminate it inside the generator, treating a deformed output as a defect to be corrected before it reaches any downstream reader, never as an input distribution to be read (Liu et al., 2024; Gillani et al., 2025; Kondo et al., 2024). The open-set rejection literature (Section 2.4) supplies energy, logit, and rank-based scores that decide "unknown," validated exclusively on natural-image OOD benchmarks — CIFAR, ImageNet, OpenOOD — where in- and out-of-distribution classes differ in object-level semantics, never in the subtle typographic geometry that separates a known Garamond from a hallucinated serif-adjacent glyph (Hofmann et al., 2024; Karunanayake et al., 2025). The representation literature (Section 2.5) supplies the disentangled, frozen, trait-localized embedding those scores require, validated on natural-image fine-grained benchmarks or on *clean* font specimens, never on probabilistically deformed crops (Wang et al., 2024; Oquab et al., 2024; Chowdhury et al., 2025; Li, Li, et al., 2024). And the measurement literature (Section 2.6) supplies deformation-robust structural distance and calibrated Top-K prediction sets, validated on image-translation and biological-classification tasks, never coupled to one another over a localized font palette under generative deformation (Zhang et al., 2024; Shi et al., 2024; Ding et al., 2025; Jiang et al., 2025).

The unifying gap is therefore not the absence of any single capability but the absence of the *connection* between them. No prior work threads attention-based text isolation (2.2) into a deformation-robust, disentangled metric embedding (2.5), reads that embedding through an open-set rejection score calibrated for typographic rather than object-level distributional shift (2.4), applied to the specific hallucinated-glyph distribution that diffusion models emit (2.3), and validated by a coupled typographic-distance-and-calibrated-Top-K protocol against a human-consensus ground truth (2.6). Each adjacent pair of clusters shares a boundary that some paper approaches from one side, but none crosses. The generative literature stops at the generator's output; the OOD literature stops at the natural-image benchmark; the metric-learning literature stops at the clean specimen; the measurement literature stops at the pixel-registered or topologically intact pair. The intersection of all five — an open-set font recognizer that maps hallucinated text crops directly onto a localized, open-source palette of the top 50–100 Google Fonts, treating deformation as its native input rather than a pre-processing failure — is the unoccupied position this thesis claims.

Two properties sharpen the boundaries of that claim. First, the target is a *localized, open-set* palette, not the full commercial typeface universe: the thesis deliberately trades exhaustive coverage for tractable, license-free reconstruction, which reframes the recognition task as ranking-plus-rejection over a bounded palette rather than retrieval over an unbounded catalog. Second, the deployment constraint is *label-free*: the class of all possible hallucinated out-of-palette glyphs is infinite and cannot be enumerated in advance, so every component — rejection score, metric head, prediction-set calibration — must operate post-hoc on representations and distributions obtainable without per-glyph ground-truth annotation from the generative domain. These two properties are not incidental scoping decisions; they are what make the intersection of the five clusters both necessary and non-trivial, and they are the constraints under which the conceptual framework of Section 2.8 is constructed.

## 2.8 Conceptual / Theoretical Framework

The framework that binds the reviewed literature into a single inference architecture is best understood as a four-layer pipeline, in which each layer resolves a limitation that the layer beneath it cannot, and the whole is evaluated against the field's own established protocol on an input distribution that protocol was never designed to accept.

**The input distribution (grounded in Section 2.3).** The framework takes as its native input the deformed glyph crop — the uncorrected output of a text-to-image diffusion model, exhibiting the micro-warping, variable kerning, stroke discontinuity, and cross-character attention smearing that Sections 2.3 documents as endemic and mechanistically intrinsic (Liu et al., 2024; Gillani et al., 2025; Kondo et al., 2024). Critically, the framework treats this distribution not as noise applied to a recoverable clean antecedent — the assumption that defines and defeats the legacy paradigm — but as the primary object of inference. There is no pristine glyph to recover; there is only a position in a continuous typographic space to locate.

**The representation layer (Section 2.5).** That position is located within a feature embedding constructed to be simultaneously disentangled, frozen, and trait-localized. A frozen, register-corrected DINOv2 backbone (Oquab et al., 2024; Darcet et al., 2024) supplies artifact-free, fine-grained patch features for thin-stroke crops on plain grounds without any label-hungry fine-tuning; a non-negative contrastive objective (Wang et al., 2024) imposes the axis-alignment that keeps typographic-identity dimensions orthogonal to deformation-artifact dimensions; class-specific prompts (Chowdhury et al., 2025) steer attention toward discriminative letterform structure and away from the semantic content of the rendered word, addressing Stroop-type interference (Li, Song, et al., 2025) at the attention level; and label-free prompt distillation (Li, Li, et al., 2024) adapts the metric head from a clean-glyph teacher to unlabeled deformed crops. The layer's defining commitment, drawn from the metric-topology literature (Huang & Zhou, 2022), is that the embedding's output is a *continuous proximity ranking* over the palette, not a categorical assignment — the ordinal structure of the distribution, not merely its argmax, is the carrier of discriminative information.

**The decision layer (Section 2.4).** Over that embedding, an open-set rejection score decides whether a crop belongs to the known palette manifold at all. Following the post-hoc, training-agnostic paradigm the OOD survey validates as the only viable option for an unbounded, un-enumerable out-of-palette class (Lu et al., 2025), the framework reads the runner-up ranking structure of the Top-K distribution as a second-order anomaly signal: an in-palette deformed crop produces the ordinal disambiguation sequence characteristic of its font's neighbors (Old Style serifs ranking other Old Style serifs), while a genuinely out-of-palette hallucination produces the incoherent cross-family ranking that signals ordinal collapse (Karunanayake et al., 2025), backstopped by energy- and logit-space scores (Hofmann et al., 2024; Djurisic et al., 2024). The Top-K shortlist is thus repurposed from a passive accuracy measure into an active rejection instrument.

**The measurement layer (Section 2.6).** Finally, the pipeline's outputs are validated by coupling two independent instruments. A re-render-and-compare typographic distance — deep-feature and region-topology structural similarity robust to the geometric misalignment between a hallucinated crop and a clean re-render (Zhang et al., 2024; Li et al., 2025) — supplies a post-prediction structural verification of the embedding's claim, while rank-calibrated, prevalence-adjusted conformal prediction (Shi et al., 2024; Ding et al., 2025) converts the raw Top-K shortlist into a coverage-guaranteed prediction set whose cardinality adapts to genuine typographic ambiguity, extended by the ControlText L2@k/cos@k distribution-matching precedent over the same Storia-AI baseline embedding this thesis adopts (Jiang et al., 2025).

**The evaluation frame (Section 2.2).** The entire pipeline is scored under DeepFont's Top-1/Top-3 accuracy protocol (Wang et al., 2015) — deliberately, because that protocol is the domain's established standard — but re-targeted from the pristine-vector regime it was built for onto the hallucinated-crop regime it categorically excluded, with the confusion structure of its errors analyzed by typographic family and its ground truth established by this study's own three-person expert-consensus panel. The conceptual contribution of the framework is exactly this recomposition: each borrowed component is theoretically at home in its source domain, but their integration — a reject-score over a disentangled frozen-ViT embedding, structurally verified and conformally calibrated, evaluated by the legacy protocol on the generative-deformation distribution — constitutes an architecture that none of the source literatures, alone or in adjacent pairs, assembles. The empirical chapters that follow test whether the theoretical transferability asserted at each layer holds under the deformation amplitudes of real, uncorrected generative AI text.
