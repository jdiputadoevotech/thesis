# Chapter 2 — Review of Related Literature

## 2.1 Introduction

This chapter reviews the literature that bounds the problem this thesis addresses and supplies the components from which its solution is assembled. The review is organized by the structure of the problem itself, in a deliberately problem-first sequence: it establishes what the established paradigm cannot do, characterizes the phenomenon that defeats it, and then assembles — component by component — the apparatus required to succeed where the paradigm fails. Five thematic clusters trace this arc. Section 2.2 reviews the paradigm this thesis is defined against: visual font recognition, from DeepFont through classical OCR and layout analysis to attention-based localization, is uniformly built on a closed-set, rigid-glyph assumption that generative AI text violates. Section 2.3 characterizes that violation — the micro-warping, variable kerning, and cross-character attention smearing this thesis terms *typographic hallucination* — which the generative literature studies only to suppress inside the generator, never to recover typeface identity from an already-deformed output. The three remaining clusters assemble the recognizer proposed for exactly that recovery task: the open-set rejection machinery that routes an out-of-palette crop to an "unknown" verdict (Section 2.4), the disentangled frozen-ViT embedding over which that rejection score is computed (Section 2.5), and the deformation-robust distance and calibrated Top-K measurement layer against which the pipeline is validated (Section 2.6). Section 2.7 synthesizes the unoccupied intersection these clusters jointly define, and Section 2.8 formalizes the conceptual framework binding them into a single inference architecture.

## 2.2 Legacy Font Recognition, OCR/Layout & Closed-Set Vector Assumptions

Visual font recognition as a scientific discipline traces to Wang, Yang, Jin, Shechtman, Aseem, and Huang's (2015) DeepFont, the first large-scale deep convolutional system for identifying typefaces from photographic inputs. DeepFont's methodological innovations were inseparable from the assumptions built into it. It trained on a synthetic corpus of clean specimens generated directly from font files, guaranteeing that the discriminative features the network learned — thick-to-thin stroke ratios, serif geometry, terminal curvature, ascender-to-x-height proportions — were uncontaminated by rendering artifacts: the model learned to read the typeface, not the noise. It was evaluated under the Top-1/Top-3 accuracy protocol that remains the field's standard, and its success validated the central claim of the clean-glyph paradigm — that a network trained on pristine synthetic specimens generalizes to real photographic inputs with commercially useful accuracy. Underlying this is a closed-set assumption in the strict sense: the hypothesis space is the set of training fonts, every input is presumed to contain a glyph whose topology was fixed at render time, and recognition is the recovery of that fixed identity from an environmental degradation channel. This thesis adopts DeepFont's Top-1/Top-3 protocol precisely because it is the established standard, but re-targets it at the morphologically unstable regime DeepFont's training methodology categorically excluded — a regime in which there is no antecedent clean glyph to recover, because signal and noise co-emerge from the same generative process.

The same presupposition governs the classical pipeline. Contemporary OCR and layout systems achieve near-ceiling performance on anatomically correct text — Ponnuru et al. (2024) report 98% accuracy for a Tesseract-based prescription-digitization pipeline under standard scanning defects, and closed-boundary layout analysis grows stable bounding regions from coherent glyph interiors (Bhunia et al., 2018). The diagnostic value of that ceiling lies not in its magnitude but in the character of the noise it tolerates: ink spread, compression, and handwriting variance perturb a character's rendering quality without dissolving its letterform — the stroke skeleton, loop closure, and descender trajectory persist beneath the corruption. Both systems implicitly compute the same function, recovering a clean underlying character by inverting the noise that obscures it, and that function is well-posed only when a pristine antecedent glyph exists. It is undefined in the diffusion regime this thesis analyzes, where the glyph in the image is not a degraded copy of a vector master but the primary output of a system with no privileged access to the intended typeface's geometry. The gap between that 98% ceiling and the performance of identical systems on generative text is the quantitative expression of the architectural assumption this thesis relaxes.

The field has itself begun migrating off the fixed geometric priors that encode this assumption at the localization step. Wang, Lv, et al.'s (2025) TransTab pairs a ViT backbone with self-attention to parse table layouts whose spatial relationships no fixed convolution kernel can describe, routing the attention-derived layout into a downstream OCR module; Sheikh et al.'s (2024) UnSupDLA extends the migration to the fully unsupervised regime, deriving layout masks from frozen self-supervised DINO encoders without labeled annotations. This migration is directly relevant to this thesis in two ways: it establishes attention-based, layout-independent feature extraction as the prerequisite for isolating glyph crops from spatially irregular generative images, and UnSupDLA's frozen-DINO, label-free strategy is a direct precedent one step upstream of the frozen-ViT embedding this thesis adopts (Section 2.5). Critically, however, this migration removes rigidity only from *localization* — neither work asks which typeface was intended when the localized text was rendered.

Two foundational surveys supply the scaffolding for the thesis's methodological choices. Sarker's (2021) deep-learning taxonomy locates the proposed recognizer within the discriminative regime, positioning the decision to freeze a self-supervised backbone and train only a lightweight metric head as a principled point in the design space rather than an ad hoc selection. Plastropoulos and Tegos (2024) make the empirical case for data augmentation as distributional coverage expansion — training on geometrically perturbed inputs pushes decision boundaries into regions stable under distortion — which licenses this thesis's synthetic-degradation strategy of subjecting clean Google Fonts specimens to elastic warping, blur, noise, and randomized tracking. Both borrowings are theoretical rather than empirical, however: neither survey addresses typography or the diffusion-deformation regime, so whether their principles transfer is an empirical question this thesis's experiments answer.

**Research Gap in the Legacy Recognition Paradigm.** The seven sources reviewed here collectively define the closed-set, geometrically rigid paradigm that constitutes the established baseline. DeepFont fixed the evaluation protocol and the clean-glyph training assumption successive systems inherited without renegotiation (Wang et al., 2015); classical OCR and layout analysis achieve near-ceiling accuracy only on topologically faithful glyphs (Ponnuru et al., 2024; Bhunia et al., 2018); the attention-based, self-supervised migration relaxes geometric rigidity at localization but leaves the morphological assumptions of font identity untouched (Sheikh et al., 2024; Wang, Lv, et al., 2025); and the taxonomy and augmentation literature licenses synthetic degradation without ever applying it to typographic recognition under generative deformation (Plastropoulos & Tegos, 2024; Sarker, 2021). None asks whether font identity can be recovered from a glyph whose topology was generated by a probabilistic latent model rather than instantiated from a vector master — a glyph in which deformation is not a noise channel to invert but an irreducible property of the generative process. Section 2.3 characterizes that process.

## 2.3 Generative AI Typographic Hallucinations & Diffusion Latent Mutations

The premise that generative AI produces morphologically unreliable text is established by the literature with unusual directness: uncorrected failure is the dominant outcome, not a tail risk. Liu et al. (2024), diagnosing visual text rendering in Stable Diffusion XL, trace the failure to an architectural limitation — the standard text encoder treats a character sequence as a semantic unit and lacks the sub-character spatial awareness needed for typographically accurate output, so that fewer than 20% of uncorrected SDXL renderings are classifiable as accurate. Their Glyph-ByT5 correction, which fine-tunes the encoder on glyph-conditioned data, raises accuracy to approximately 90%, confirming the failure is architectural rather than fundamental; for this thesis the relevant datum is the uncorrected baseline, in which deformation is the default output state. Li, Song, et al. (2025) compound this from the perception side: state-of-the-art vision-language models (VLMs) are readily derailed by a Stroop-type semantic interference, in which the lexical meaning of the rendered word overrides the model's reading of the visual typeface, and neither few-shot exemplars nor chain-of-thought reliably suppresses it. Because this effect operates even on *clean*, legible text, semantic distraction and glyph deformation are independently sufficient to defeat general-purpose recognition — closing off off-the-shelf VLMs as a solution and motivating a dedicated visual-texture pipeline architecturally isolated from lexical content.

How the deformation arises has been localized, with increasing precision, in the attention structure of the generator. Gillani et al.'s (2025) TextPixs identifies uncontrolled cross-character attention as the proximate cause: during denoising, a character's attention map bleeds into the spatial footprints of adjacent characters, so that neighboring letterforms pull one another's strokes off-axis (micro-warping), spacing is synthesized over conflated representations (variable kerning), and strongly co-attending glyphs partially merge. Their character-aware attention-segregation loss reduces Character Error Rate from 0.21 to 0.08 and Word Error Rate from 0.25 to 0.15; the baseline figures are measurements of the uncorrected mechanism this thesis's pipeline must read. Du et al.'s (2025) TextCrafter extends the account to multi-instance scenes, where the latent representations of co-present text strings entangle across cross-attention layers and blend typographic properties; their CVTG-2K catalogue of 2,000 stress prompts — spanning text position, instance count, sequence length, and attribute diversity — is an empirical map of the deformation conditions this thesis's synthetic pipeline is calibrated to reproduce through elastic warping, tracking randomization, and kerning jitter.

This deformation is neither uniform nor incidental to the model class. Zhuang et al. (2025) show, via their HDGlyph framework, that degradation concentrates in the typographic long tail — rare glyphs, small text, CJK characters — with rendering gains from correction of roughly 5% on English and 12% on Chinese. The implication for a localized palette is acute and counterintuitive: the display, script, and monospace faces that a clean-glyph recognizer would find *most* distinctive are precisely those most severely hallucinated, so the recognizer must exercise the greatest caution exactly where it would otherwise be most confident. Kondo et al. (2024) supply peer-reviewed confirmation from the generation side that diffusion models represent font style not as discrete catalogued entities but as positions on a continuous latent manifold: interpolating between two known styles yields intermediate glyphs corresponding to no named typeface. Generative text is therefore not a perturbed copy of a canonical face but a morphologically novel entity sampled from continuous latent coordinates — which is precisely why this thesis's identifier must operate on continuous typographic distance rather than categorical matching. Finally, Shu et al. (2025) supply the cross-cutting evaluation taxonomy (VTPBench/VTPScore) that situates the dispersed metrics above within a single measurement frame, against which this thesis positions its own typeface-recoverability protocol as addressing a dimension existing infrastructure does not cover.

**Research Gap in the Generative Hallucination Literature.** The seven sources here establish that typographic hallucination is endemic (Li, Song, et al., 2025; Liu et al., 2024), mechanistically characterized at the level of cross-character attention (Du et al., 2025; Gillani et al., 2025), concentrated in the long tail (Zhuang et al., 2025), intrinsic to the continuous latent structure of diffusion models (Kondo et al., 2024), and measurable within a unifying taxonomy (Shu et al., 2025). Yet every source shares an objective that is the inverse of this thesis's: each studies deformation in order to *suppress* it inside the generator, evaluating whether a corrected pipeline emits less-deformed output, with OCR-centric metrics (CER, WER, VTPScore) that measure whether the correct *characters* appear — never whether the *typeface identity* survives in recoverable form. Treating the documented deformation distribution not as a defect to eliminate but as the *input distribution* of a font identifier is the unoccupied intersection between this literature and the open-set, metric-embedding, and structural-distance literature that follows.

## 2.4 Logit-Space Thresholds & Open-Set Rejection Scores

Sections 2.2 and 2.3 establish complementary halves of the problem: the closed-set paradigm cannot represent inputs outside its training distribution, and generative pipelines emit exactly such inputs by default. This section reviews the inferential machinery that converts that gap into a functioning rejection decision — how a trained classifier detects that an input lies outside its training manifold and routes it to an "unknown" bin rather than forcing a closed-set label. The literature organizes this into a consistent two-component framework: a *rejection score*, computed from the model's internal representations, and a *threshold* against which it is compared. The central question is where in the network the most informative score lives.

Two influential post-hoc scores operate on the output layer. Hofmann et al. (2024) ground their Energy-based Hopfield Boosting (EHB) in the energy formalism of modern Hopfield networks: inputs near the dense training manifold receive low energy, while out-of-distribution (OOD) inputs in the sparse interstitial regions receive high energy, and an outlier-exposure scheme that mines *boundary-adjacent* auxiliary outliers resharpens the energy gradient exactly where the decision is least certain. EHB reports state-of-the-art False Positive Rate at 95% recall (e.g., improving ImageNet-1K from 50.74% to 36.60%), a metric with direct operational relevance — it measures the fraction of genuine OOD crops passed as recognized fonts at a threshold recovering 95% of true in-palette examples. Djurisic et al.'s (2024) Logit Scaling (LTS) instead operates on the raw logit vector, deriving a sample-specific normalization scalar from the input's features to make scoring invariant to the global magnitude variation that confounds naive maximum-logit detectors while preserving the discriminative ordinal structure. LTS is post-hoc, architecture-agnostic, and — critically for a thesis with no font-domain OOD training examples — requires no auxiliary outliers or retraining. Together these define the principal axes of post-hoc detection: energy scores responding to manifold geometry and logit scores responding to activation magnitude. Both share a limitation from this thesis's vantage: neither has been evaluated where in- and out-of-distribution classes differ only in subtle typographic geometry, where the distance between a known Garamond and a hallucinated serif-adjacent glyph may be smaller than that between CIFAR-10's automobile and truck.

A complementary line argues that the *full ordinal structure* of the logit vector, not just its maximum, carries the open-set signal. Karunanayake et al.'s (2025) ExCeL observes that on in-distribution inputs the ranking of runner-up classes is highly deterministic — reflecting the learned feature-space adjacency, so that related classes reliably rank as one another's nearest competitors — whereas on OOD inputs this ordinal determinism collapses into noise. Fusing the *extreme* signal of the maximum logit with the *collective* signal of runner-up rankings yields a score more discriminative than either alone, and ExCeL's demonstrated strength in the near-OOD regime — visually similar but distinct classes — is the closest natural-image analogue to typographic OOD. This maps exactly onto this thesis's Top-K protocol: a genuinely in-palette deformed crop should produce a runner-up sequence dominated by typographically adjacent faces (a Garamond crop surfacing other Old Style serifs), while an out-of-palette hallucination produces an incoherent cross-family ranking. The Top-K shortlist is thereby repurposed from a passive accuracy measure into an active anomaly signal — a precedent ExCeL proves in the natural-image domain and this thesis extends to typography. Lu et al.'s (2025) task-oriented survey situates all three methods within the training-agnostic (post-hoc) category and confirms this constraint is not an engineering compromise but the only viable option here: the out-of-palette class — all possible hallucinated glyphs outside the 50–100 fonts — is infinite and cannot be enumerated, sampled, or labeled in advance, so any viable scheme must derive its signal post-hoc from a pre-trained classifier's output, calibrating its threshold on held-out in-palette crops alone.

**Research Gap in Open-Set Rejection.** The four sources supply a mature framework: energy-based manifold scores (Hofmann et al., 2024), architecture-agnostic logit rescaling (Djurisic et al., 2024), runner-up-ranking anomaly signals (Karunanayake et al., 2025), and a survey situating all three in the validated post-hoc paradigm (Lu et al., 2025). The gap is precise: every benchmark is a natural-image OOD task — CIFAR, ImageNet, OpenOOD — where classes differ in object-level semantics, texture, or scene structure. None is evaluated on *typographic* OOD: whether the energy gradient at a font-family boundary is steep enough for reliable rejection, whether logit-magnitude normalization captures deformation-induced scale variability, and whether runner-up ranking collapses measurably for hallucinated glyphs remain open questions — answered only over the metric embedding of Section 2.5 and the validation protocols of Section 2.6.

## 2.5 Feature Disentanglement & Frozen-ViT Metric Embedding

Every decision the rejection layer makes is only as reliable as the geometry of the embedding the scores are computed in. If a Garamond and a Lato specimen are not separated by a steep energy gradient, EHB fails at typographic granularity; if LTS's magnitude normalization conflates a single font's deformation variance with between-family variance, it suppresses the signal; if ExCeL's runner-up rankings are not ordinal-stable for in-palette crops, their collapse for hallucinated crops carries no information. This section reviews the literature governing that representation layer: an embedding that is simultaneously *disentangled* (axis-aligned, so typographic-identity dimensions do not entangle with deformation artifacts), *frozen* (from a self-supervised backbone, not fine-tuned on fonts), and *trait-localized* (steered to attend to letterform structure rather than lexical content).

Axis-alignment is the first precondition. In unconstrained contrastive embeddings, deformation-artifact dimensions can distort typographic-identity dimensions enough to carry a deformed sans-serif crop across a family boundary, making the rejection problem ill-posed. Wang et al. (2024) address this at its root with Non-negative Contrastive Learning (NCL), which imposes a non-negative matrix factorization–style constraint on contrastive features; the associated identifiability result yields sparse, axis-aligned features whose dimensions correspond to interpretable visual attributes, outperforming standard contrastive learning on disentanglement and downstream classification. Applied to typography, this keeps the axes encoding serif geometry, stroke contrast, and proportion orthogonal to those encoding warp and blur — so a deformed serif crop accumulates deformation-axis activation without its identity-axis activation shifting across a family boundary, the formal precondition for a stable energy gradient at typographic granularity. Whether the constraint transfers, with typographic sub-features as the recovered "parts," is an empirical question this thesis is positioned to answer.

The backbone requires independent justification. Oquab et al.'s (2024) DINOv2 is a self-supervised Vision Transformer trained by label-free distillation whose features transfer to fine-grained classification, segmentation, and retrieval without domain-specific fine-tuning; its decisive property here is quality under *complete freezing* — linear-probe evaluations show fine-grained discrimination is already encoded in the frozen features — so a lightweight NCL metric head over frozen patch embeddings can access typographic structure without the label-hungry cost of fine-tuning. Its emergent attention further groups image regions into coherent parts without part supervision, matching this thesis's need for local, patch-level anatomical features (terminals, bowl junctions, counters) rather than a global word gestalt. One correction is prerequisite: Darcet et al. (2024) document that self-supervised ViTs, DINOv2 included, repurpose low-information background tokens as high-norm "artifact" registers that corrupt dense features — a pathology most severe in exactly the sparse-signal glyph-crop regime, where discriminative strokes occupy a small area on a plain ground. Their dedicated *register* tokens absorb this global storage, restoring clean artifact-free patch features for thin-stroke crops. DINOv2's frozen fine-grained features (Oquab et al., 2024) plus register correction (Darcet et al., 2024) thus define the backbone on which the NCL objective (Wang et al., 2024) and the following trait-localization mechanisms operate.

A clean, disentangled space is necessary but not sufficient: a general-purpose backbone encodes the rendered word's *semantic content* alongside its typographic structure — the very Stroop interference Li, Song, et al. (2025) document (Section 2.3). Chowdhury et al.'s (2025) Prompt-CAM steers a frozen ViT with class-specific learnable prompts that must attend to the patches uniquely discriminative for each class; validated across twelve fine-grained domains, it yields attention maps localized to domain-relevant parts. For typography, a Garamond prompt attends to bracketed serifs, moderate stroke contrast, and proportional ratios while ignoring whichever word is rendered — routing the class-discriminative attention path through typographic-structure patches rather than semantic ones, addressing Stroop interference at the attention level and at negligible parameter cost. Because these prompts read local features wherever the strokes fall, the mechanism is layout-independent, accommodating the kerning and spacing irregularity of generative crops in a way fixed-receptive-field convolutions cannot. Adapting this metric head to a localized palette without per-glyph labels — none exist for in-the-wild generative crops — is solved by the principle behind Li, Li, et al.'s (2024) PromptKD, which distills a teacher into a student via learnable prompts by aligning logit distributions over *unlabeled* domain images through Kullback–Leibler divergence. A teacher trained on clean Google Fonts supplies the reference distribution; the student, fed abundant unlabeled generative crops, learns prompts mapping deformed inputs to the same logit regions as their clean counterparts — a label-free pathway across the clean-to-deformed domain shift. Underlying all three mechanisms is a claim from the deep metric-learning and feature-topology literature (Huang & Zhou, 2022): the mapping from input to class should be a continuous function to *proximity rankings* in a structured metric space, not a categorical assignment. A crop deformed off every font's clean manifold still occupies a position encoding soft proximity — how close to Garamond relative to Crimson relative to EB Garamond — that a hard argmax discards. This continuous-ranking view is the representational foundation on which the Top-K protocol, the structural distances of Section 2.6, and ExCeL-style anomaly detection (Section 2.4) all converge.

**Research Gap in the Representation Layer.** The six sources constitute the representational foundation: disentangled contrastive features (Wang et al., 2024), a frozen fine-grained ViT (Oquab et al., 2024), register-based artifact correction (Darcet et al., 2024), trait-localizing prompts (Chowdhury et al., 2025), label-free prompt distillation (Li, Li, et al., 2024), and a metric-topology framework licensing continuous proximity ranking (Huang & Zhou, 2022). The gap is empirical: every validation context is a natural-image fine-grained benchmark or a *clean* typographic one. No study reports results for the deformed regime — glyphs probabilistically altered by a diffusion model's latent process, exhibiting warp, stroke discontinuity, and cross-character smearing. Whether frozen DINOv2 features remain separable at the family boundary under real deformation amplitudes, whether Prompt-CAM localizes when discriminative patches are disrupted by warp and blur, and whether PromptKD yields a metric head faithful enough to support ExCeL-style ordinal-stability detection are precisely the questions this thesis's embedding experiments answer.

## 2.6 Advanced Visual Distance Metrics (SSIM) & Human-Proxy Ground-Truth Alignment

Three of the four pipeline components are now assembled: the deformation distribution (Section 2.3), the rejection score (Section 2.4), and the embedding it computes over (Section 2.5). What remains is the *measurement layer* that assesses output quality against an empirical standard — the typographic distance metric defining "closeness" to the correct font, the calibration mechanism turning a Top-K shortlist into a warranted uncertainty claim, and the ground-truth methodology against which both are judged.

The distance side must overcome a foundational obstacle. Classical SSIM (Wang et al., 2004) assesses similarity by comparing luminance, contrast, and structure at *corresponding pixel coordinates* — appropriate when a degraded image is a spatially registered copy of its reference, but collapsing under the comparison this thesis requires: a clean re-rendered specimen against a hallucinated crop whose warping, boundary blur, and variable kerning have displaced typographic features from their reference positions. Classical SSIM penalizes those shifts as dissimilarity even when typeface identity is intact. Zhang et al.'s (2024) DeepSSIM reconstitutes the measure in deep feature space, where a trained encoder represents structural properties at an abstraction not anchored to pixel coordinates — an elastically warped serif terminal still occupies a nearby feature-space region — and adds an attention-calibration step correcting residual positional offsets; the co-authorship of Zhou Wang, originator of SSIM, provides direct lineage. For this thesis, DeepSSIM operationalizes typographic distance through a re-render-and-compare loop: the predicted font re-renders the detected word, and its structural similarity to the original crop is computed in feature space, resistant to the spatial displacement that defeats pixel SSIM and specific to typographic structure rather than character identity (CER/WER). Li, Chen, et al. (2025) supply a methodologically independent grounding with SAMScore, which measures structural correspondence over Segment Anything Model embeddings — comparing *content structure*, the topology of coherent regions, rather than pixel intensities or classification features — and rates best across 19 image-translation tasks; the co-authorship of Alan Bovik places it in the same SSIM lineage. SAMScore captures the structural fact that a bowl and a stroke exist and connect, independent of their pixel coordinates, giving a region-topology distance complementary to DeepSSIM's boundary-and-texture similarity; applied jointly they triangulate typographic similarity from two registration-free perspectives. Umer et al. (2022), in a soft-computing treatment of robust classification over high-variance arrays, supply the theoretical license for treating structural distance as a continuous function over geometrically disrupted inputs — justifying the softmax-weighted, continuous structural-distance computation this thesis uses and the inferential step from a single DeepSSIM/SAMScore value to a cross-pair ranking by typographic proximity.

The distance metric measures how close a prediction is, not how confident the system should be. A fixed-K shortlist treats all predictions with equal certainty, conflating a crop at a font cluster's center (Top-1 already reliable) with one stranded between families (true font perhaps absent from any three-element set). Shi et al.'s (2024) RC3P — Rank-Calibrated Class-Conditional Conformal Prediction — constructs the smallest class set guaranteed at a specified confidence to contain the true class, using label-rank calibration to deliver *class-conditional* coverage (each class independently meeting the guarantee) where standard conformal prediction achieves only marginal coverage, reducing average set size by roughly 26.25%. Transferred here, RC3P converts the classifier's softmax over the palette into a coverage-guaranteed prediction set whose cardinality varies by input — a small set near a cluster center, a larger one in an interstitial region — each shortlist carrying a formal confidence claim a downstream reconstruction pipeline can act on. Ding et al. (2025) extend conformal prediction to the long tail that Section 2.3 flags as hardest, introducing a prevalence-adjusted softmax score that compensates for the underconfidence of rare classes; validated on Pl@ntNet-300K and iNaturalist — whose heavy imbalance mirrors a palette where common humanist sans-serifs vastly outnumber rare display and script faces — it maintains per-class coverage that standard calibration misrepresents. Together, RC3P (Shi et al., 2024) and its long-tail extension (Ding et al., 2025) guarantee coverage across the full typographic range this thesis's deformation-robustness claim must span. Jiang et al.'s (2025) ControlText supplies the typography-native precedent for evaluating predictions by *distribution*, not binary top-K: measuring font fidelity through L2@k and cos@k between the top-k classifier probability vectors of reference and generated images, using the Storia-AI Google Font Classifier (3,474 fonts) as the reference embedding. This is doubly relevant — it templates the Top-K distribution-matching this thesis extends, and its reference classifier is the same Storia-AI `font-classify/` baseline this thesis adopts, an independent, contemporaneous confirmation that this baseline is the field's standard reference embedding.

The ground truth against which these calibrated sets and distribution scores are judged is this study's own human-proxy methodology, not a cited source: a three-person expert consensus panel independently assigns a ground-truth typeface — or an "unknown / hallucinated" verdict — to a sample of [N] generative crops, with agreement among at least two of three fixing the label. This original methodological contribution adopts the class-conditional coverage criterion of the conformal literature (Ding et al., 2025; Shi et al., 2024) as its evaluation target — a prediction set is correct if and only if the consensus label is among its elements — and produces an independent human estimate of the prevalence and severity of genuine typographic ambiguity in uncorrected generative outputs.

**Research Gap in the Measurement Infrastructure.** The six sources establish the required infrastructure but do not occupy this thesis's measurement position. DeepSSIM (Zhang et al., 2024), SAMScore (Li et al., 2025), and the structural-distance optimization framework (Umer et al., 2022) supply deformation-robust distance validated on image-quality and translation benchmarks — never the clean-re-render-versus-hallucinated-crop comparison. RC3P (Shi et al., 2024) and its long-tail extension (Ding et al., 2025) supply calibrated prediction sets validated on natural-image and biological classification — never a font palette under generative deformation — and ControlText's L2@k/cos@k (Jiang et al., 2025) is validated on clean or controlled renders. No prior work couples a deformation-robust structural distance with a calibrated Top-K set over a localized open-set Google Fonts palette, assessed against a human-consensus ground truth built for in-the-wild generative crops. That unified protocol is the measurement contribution of this work.

## 2.7 Synthesis and Research Gap

The five clusters reviewed here are individually mature, each internally complete — yet read together they describe a set of capabilities never assembled into a single pipeline aimed at a single problem, and the gap this thesis occupies is defined precisely by the seams between them. The legacy paradigm (Section 2.2) supplies an evaluation protocol and closed-set architecture that presuppose topological fidelity; its own migration toward attention-based, self-supervised localization (Sheikh et al., 2024; Wang, Lv, et al., 2025) removes geometric rigidity from *localization* while leaving the morphological assumptions of *font identity* intact. The generative-hallucination literature (Section 2.3) characterizes, with increasing mechanistic precision, exactly the deformation that violates those assumptions — but studies it only to eliminate it inside the generator, never as an input distribution to be read (Gillani et al., 2025; Kondo et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024). The open-set literature (Section 2.4) supplies energy, logit, and rank-based rejection scores validated exclusively on natural-image OOD, where classes differ in object-level semantics rather than typographic geometry (Hofmann et al., 2024; Karunanayake et al., 2025). The representation literature (Section 2.5) supplies the disentangled, frozen, trait-localized embedding those scores require, validated on natural-image fine-grained benchmarks or clean fonts, never deformed crops (Chowdhury et al., 2025; Li, Li, et al., 2024; Oquab et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024). And the measurement literature (Section 2.6) supplies deformation-robust distance and calibrated Top-K sets, never coupled over a localized palette under generative deformation (Ding et al., 2025; Jiang et al., 2025; Shi et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024).

The unifying gap is thus not the absence of any single capability but the absence of the *connection* between them. No prior work threads attention-based text isolation (Section 2.2) into a deformation-robust disentangled embedding (Section 2.5), reads it through an open-set score calibrated for typographic rather than object-level shift (Section 2.4), applies this to the specific hallucinated-glyph distribution diffusion models emit (Section 2.3), and validates it by a coupled typographic-distance-and-calibrated-Top-K protocol against a human-consensus ground truth (Section 2.6). Each adjacent pair shares a boundary some paper approaches from one side, but none crosses. Two properties sharpen the claim. First, the target is a *localized, open-set* palette, not the commercial typeface universe: the thesis trades exhaustive coverage for tractable, license-free reconstruction, reframing recognition as ranking-plus-rejection over a bounded palette. Second, the deployment is *label-free*: the class of out-of-palette hallucinations is infinite and unenumerable, so every component must operate post-hoc on representations obtainable without per-glyph generative-domain annotation. These constraints are what make the five-cluster intersection both necessary and non-trivial, and they frame the conceptual framework of Section 2.8.

## 2.8 Conceptual / Theoretical Framework

The framework binding the reviewed literature into a single inference architecture is a four-layer pipeline, each layer resolving a limitation the one beneath it cannot, evaluated against the field's own protocol on an input distribution that protocol was never designed to accept.

**The input distribution (Section 2.3).** The framework takes as its native input the deformed glyph crop — the uncorrected output of a text-to-image diffusion model, exhibiting the micro-warping, variable kerning, stroke discontinuity, and cross-character attention smearing documented as endemic and mechanistically intrinsic (Gillani et al., 2025; Kondo et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024). Crucially, it treats this not as noise applied to a recoverable clean antecedent — the assumption that defines and defeats the legacy paradigm — but as the primary object of inference: there is no pristine glyph to recover, only a position in a continuous typographic space to locate.

**The representation layer (Section 2.5).** That position is located within an embedding built to be disentangled, frozen, and trait-localized. A frozen, register-corrected DINOv2 backbone (Darcet et al., 2024; Oquab et al., 2024) supplies artifact-free fine-grained patch features for thin-stroke crops without fine-tuning; a non-negative contrastive objective (Wang et al., 2024) keeps typographic-identity dimensions orthogonal to deformation artifacts; class-specific prompts (Chowdhury et al., 2025) steer attention toward letterform structure and away from lexical content, addressing Stroop interference (Li, Song, et al., 2025) at the attention level; and label-free prompt distillation (Li, Li, et al., 2024) adapts the metric head from a clean-glyph teacher to unlabeled deformed crops. Its defining commitment, from the metric-topology literature (Huang & Zhou, 2022), is that the output is a *continuous proximity ranking* over the palette — the distribution's ordinal structure, not its argmax, carries the discriminative information.

**The decision layer (Section 2.4).** Over that embedding, an open-set rejection score decides whether a crop belongs to the known palette at all. Following the post-hoc, training-agnostic paradigm the OOD survey validates as the only viable option for an unbounded, unenumerable out-of-palette class (Lu et al., 2025), the framework reads the runner-up ranking structure of the Top-K distribution as a second-order anomaly signal — an in-palette crop produces the ordinal disambiguation sequence characteristic of its font's neighbors, while a genuine hallucination produces incoherent cross-family ranking (Karunanayake et al., 2025) — backstopped by energy- and logit-space scores (Djurisic et al., 2024; Hofmann et al., 2024). The Top-K shortlist is thus repurposed from a passive accuracy measure into an active rejection instrument.

**The measurement layer (Section 2.6).** Finally, the outputs are validated by coupling two independent instruments: a re-render-and-compare typographic distance robust to the misalignment between a hallucinated crop and a clean re-render (Li et al., 2025; Zhang et al., 2024) supplies post-prediction structural verification, while rank-calibrated, prevalence-adjusted conformal prediction (Ding et al., 2025; Shi et al., 2024) converts the raw shortlist into a coverage-guaranteed set whose cardinality adapts to genuine ambiguity, extended by the ControlText L2@k/cos@k precedent over the same Storia-AI baseline this thesis adopts (Jiang et al., 2025).

The entire pipeline is scored under DeepFont's Top-1/Top-3 accuracy protocol (Wang et al., 2015) — deliberately, because it is the domain standard — but re-targeted from the pristine-vector regime onto the hallucinated-crop regime it categorically excluded, its errors analyzed by typographic family and its ground truth fixed by this study's three-person expert-consensus panel. The conceptual contribution is exactly this recomposition: each borrowed component is at home in its source domain, but their integration — a reject-score over a disentangled frozen-ViT embedding, structurally verified and conformally calibrated, evaluated by the legacy protocol on the generative-deformation distribution — is an architecture none of the source literatures assembles. The empirical chapters test whether the transferability asserted at each layer holds under the deformation amplitudes of real, uncorrected generative text.
