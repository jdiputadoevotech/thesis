## 2.2 Legacy Font Recognition, OCR/Layout & Closed-Set Vector Assumptions

### A.1 Domain Anchor: DeepFont and the Closed-Set Clean-Glyph Paradigm

The trajectory of visual font recognition as a scientific discipline traces to a single landmark contribution: Wang, Yang, Jin, Shechtman, Aseem, and Huang's (2015) DeepFont system, the first large-scale deep convolutional framework purpose-built for identifying typefaces from photographic image inputs. DeepFont's methodological innovations were inseparable from the assumptions built into its design. Training proceeded on a synthetic corpus of clean, programmatically rendered specimens — each character generated directly from the corresponding font file, yielding samples of metrically uniform stroke width, precisely controlled inter-character spacing, and zero topological ambiguity. This synthetic data strategy was not a compromise born of label scarcity but a deliberate design choice: generating training images from font files guaranteed that the discriminative features the CNN would learn — the ratio of thick to thin strokes, the geometry of serifs, the curvature of round terminals, the proportional relationships between ascenders and x-heights — were uncontaminated by rendering artifacts. The model learned to read the typeface, not the noise.

Performance was evaluated under the Top-1 and Top-3 accuracy protocol, which remains the standard benchmark for font identification to the present day. Under this protocol, a recognition is correct at Top-1 if the highest-scored class matches the ground-truth font, and correct at Top-3 if the ground truth appears among the three highest-scored classes. DeepFont's demonstrated success under this protocol validated the central claim of the clean-glyph paradigm: that a convolutional network trained on pristine synthetic specimens could generalize to real-world photographic inputs — scanned typeset pages, cropped magazine headlines, photographed storefronts — with commercially useful accuracy.

The theoretical underpinning of this success is a signal decomposition that clean-glyph training enforces: the discriminative representation learned by the CNN separates the *typographic identity signal* (which font) from the *environmental degradation channel* (how it was photographed). Under this decomposition, the recognizer's task is well-defined and bounded — every input image is presumed to contain a glyph whose topology was fixed at render time, and the question the network answers is which of the known fonts produced the observed topology. This is a closed-set assumption in the strict sense: the hypothesis space is the set of fonts in the training corpus, and every input is presumed to belong to exactly one element of that set.

The implications of this assumption for the present thesis are precise. When a generative AI model renders text, it does not copy a glyph from a font file and degrade it through a noise channel. Instead, it constructs a pixel arrangement whose local structure approximates the intended character through a probabilistic denoising process that is inherently topology-agnostic. The resulting image may visually resemble a typeface — it may even pass casual visual inspection — but it does not share the parametric identity of any vector master. DeepFont's signal decomposition fails at the first step: there is no recoverable typographic identity signal to separate from the noise, because the noise and the signal co-emerge from the same generative process. The present thesis adopts DeepFont's Top-1 and Top-3 accuracy protocol as its performance framework precisely because that protocol is the field's established standard, but it re-targets the entire pipeline at the morphologically unstable, topology-uncertain regime that DeepFont's training methodology categorically excluded.

### A.2 Classical OCR and Geometric Layout Analysis: The Rigid-Glyph Assumption in Practice

The assumptions encoded in DeepFont's synthetic training protocol are not idiosyncratic; they reproduce, in a deep learning context, the same structural presupposition that governs the entire classical pipeline for optical character recognition and document layout analysis. Classical OCR and layout analysis achieve their highest performance on text that is anatomically correct — text in which each character preserves the topological contracts of its letterform — and that success is the clearest possible illustration of how performance ceilings encode architectural assumptions.

The contemporary OCR literature provides a representative benchmark. Authors of ICMLDE (2024) implement a Tesseract-based OCR pipeline with adaptive thresholding to extract and digitize handwritten and smudged prescription data — a corpus that exhibits substantial within-sample variability in ink density, letterform idiosyncrasy, paper texture, and scanning compression. The reported accuracy of 98% on standard document scanning defects is an instructive figure, and its diagnostic value lies not in its magnitude but in what it reveals about the *character* of the noise that the system handles. Every defect tested — ink spread, scanner noise, smudging, handwriting variance — perturbs the *rendering quality* of a character without dissolving the underlying letterform structure. The stroke skeleton persists through ink spread; the loop closure persists through compression; the descender trajectory persists through handwriting idiosyncrasy. Adaptive thresholding and morphological cleaning recover the character because the character's topological identity — the contracted set of spatial relationships that define a letter — is not destroyed by these noise processes, only obscured. The 98% ceiling is achievable because the problem is one of signal recovery, and the signal is structurally intact beneath the corruption.

At the level of document layout, the same presupposition manifests as a commitment to rule-based geometric analysis. The classical approach to text-region detection, documented in IEEE Dataset/Architecture Authors (2018), treats each character or word block as an object with predictable, stable boundaries: a convex hull, a bounding box, or a connected-component region grown from a seed point under the assumption that the glyph interior is locally coherent. Bounding-box estimation algorithms of this class work because intentional inscription — mechanical printing, typesetting, or skilled handwriting — respects the topological contracts that define legible letterforms: loops are closed, terminals are stable, strokes do not bifurcate randomly. The classical closed-boundary analysis is thus an accurate computational model of its target phenomenon, but the target phenomenon is a restricted class of inputs: those in which human or mechanical agency guarantees that the relationship between a glyph and its intended typeface is one of faithful instantiation.

Together, Tesseract-based OCR (Authors of ICMLDE, 2024) and closed-boundary layout analysis (IEEE Dataset/Architecture Authors, 2018) define the prior art on text-in-image processing for anatomically correct glyphs. Their diagnostic utility as prior-art anchors for this thesis is precisely that their failure mode is structurally legible. Both systems implicitly compute the same function: given an input image, recover the clean underlying character by inverting the noise that obscures it. This function is well-posed when the clean character exists as an antecedent state — when there is a pristine glyph that the noise was applied to. It is undefined when the noise and the signal co-emerged from a probabilistic generation process, as in the diffusion model regime that produces the generative AI text crops this thesis analyzes. In that regime, there is no antecedent clean glyph to recover; the glyph that appears in the image is not a degraded copy of a pristine rendering but the primary output of a system with no privileged access to the intended typeface's geometry. The 98% accuracy ceiling, far from being a standard to aspire to, is the performance achievable on a categorically different problem — and the gap between that ceiling and the performance of identical systems on generative AI text is the quantitative expression of the architectural assumption this thesis is designed to relax.

### A.3 The Attention-Based Localization Bridge: Migration From Fixed Receptive Fields

The recognition that fixed CNN receptive fields impose inappropriate spatial priors for irregular text layouts has driven a sustained migration toward attention-based alternatives. This migration is the architectural precondition for any downstream font-identification system that must operate on in-the-wild generative AI outputs, where text may appear at arbitrary scales, orientations, and densities, and where the spatial structure of the text region is not recoverable from a geometric prior.

Wang, Lv, Wu, Xie, Lu, Wang, Zhan, and Su (2025) illustrate the upstream half of this migration in the domain of table detection and tabular data extraction from scanned document images. Their TransTab framework integrates a ViT backbone with self-attention to parse layout structures whose spatial relationships span variable-width columns, irregular cell merges, and multi-row headers that cannot be described by a convolution kernel of any fixed size. The self-attention mechanism processes all spatial positions jointly, allowing the model to condition its representation of any one layout element on the global context of the surrounding structure — a capability that fixed-receptive-field CNNs can approximate only by stacking many layers at the cost of exponentially growing parameter counts. TransTab then routes the attention-derived layout representation into an EasyOCR sub-module for character digitization, establishing a modular pipeline in which text localization (a spatial organization problem) and character recognition (a perceptual identity problem) are handled by architecturally distinct components whose outputs compose cleanly.

The relevance of TransTab to this thesis is architectural rather than domain-specific. The argument that Wang, Lv, et al. (2025) make — that a ViT with self-attention can model spatial structure that rigid CNN receptive fields cannot — transfers directly to the glyph-crop isolation problem that precedes font identification. A generative AI output is not a document with conventional typesetting; it is an image in which text regions may occupy arbitrary spatial configurations, where character baselines may drift, kerning may be irregular, and the separation between text foreground and pictorial background may not respect the binarization thresholds that classical OCR pre-processing assumes. Attention-based feature extraction, reading layout relationships at multiple spatial scales simultaneously, is the architectural prerequisite for isolating individual glyph crops from such images without imposing the rigid geometric priors that classical layout analysis requires.

Sheikh, Shehzadi, Hashmi, Stricker, and Afzal (2024) extend this migration to the fully unsupervised regime with UnSupDLA, a document layout analysis framework that eliminates the need for labeled bounding-box annotations by deriving layout masks from frozen self-supervised DINO encoders. UnSupDLA employs a loss reduction technique — formalized as $L_{\text{drop}}$ — to expand the exploration of layout region boundaries beyond what a supervised model constrained to annotated regions would discover, and refines the resulting pseudo-masks across multiple iterations to converge on reliable structural boundaries without ever consulting a ground-truth annotation. The methodology is label-free by design, motivated by the practical observation that annotated layout datasets are expensive to produce and rapidly become stale as document formats evolve.

The implications for this thesis are twofold. First, UnSupDLA's use of frozen DINO features for text-region segmentation establishes a direct precedent for the frozen-ViT approach this thesis adopts at the font-identification stage — the same encoder family, the same self-supervised pre-training, applied one step upstream in the same overall pipeline. The architectural continuity between unsupervised layout isolation (UnSupDLA) and self-supervised feature embedding (DINOv2, Section 2.5) is not incidental; it reflects the principled commitment to representations that do not require per-sample annotation, a commitment justified by the no-label constraint governing in-the-wild generative AI crops. Second, the pseudo-mask refinement paradigm of UnSupDLA provides methodological license for iterative, self-supervised training strategies — the only available option when the input distribution consists of generative AI outputs for which no font ground-truth exists.

The transition traced by TransTab and UnSupDLA — from fixed-receptive-field CNN localization to attention-based, self-supervised layout isolation — is thus the field's own acknowledgment that rigid geometric priors are insufficient for the spatially irregular text environments that constitute the real-world deployment scenario of this thesis. Critically, that acknowledgment does not extend to the font-identity step: these papers localize text regions and recognize characters, but neither asks which typeface was intended when the localized text was rendered. The present thesis occupies exactly the gap this localization migration opens: it accepts the modern, attention-based localization apparatus as upstream infrastructure and asks, for the first time, whether font identity can be recovered from the glyph crops produced when that infrastructure is applied to generative AI outputs.

### A.4 Deep Learning Taxonomy and the Synthetic-Degradation Rationale

The architectural and methodological choices of this thesis — the selection of a discriminative metric-learning framework, the use of synthetic data with algorithmic degradation, and the training strategy combining clean specimens with distortion-augmented variants — require a theoretical scaffolding that locates these decisions within the established landscape of deep learning practice. Two foundational surveys supply this scaffolding.

Sarker (2021) provides the comprehensive taxonomy that organizes deep learning architectures along three primary axes: discriminative models, which learn the mapping from input space to label space; generative models, which learn the joint or marginal distribution of the data; and hybrid architectures, which combine both objectives within a single framework. The font recognizer proposed by this thesis operates within the discriminative regime: its function is to map a hallucinated glyph crop to a probability distribution over a closed palette of known typefaces, with an additional channel for open-set rejection when the crop falls outside the palette boundary. Sarker's treatment of convolutional feature hierarchies, attention mechanisms, transfer learning, and hyperparameter optimization provides the authoritative terminological and conceptual framework within which the architectural decisions of this thesis — specifically, the choice to freeze a self-supervised backbone and train only a lightweight metric head — can be positioned as a principled point in the discriminative design space rather than an ad hoc engineering selection.

Plastropoulos and Tegos (2024) complement the taxonomy with an empirical and theoretical case for data augmentation as a representation-shaping mechanism. Reviewing machine learning and deep learning approaches for object detection, semantic segmentation, and human action recognition, Plastropoulos and Tegos demonstrate that combining classical image pre-processing — noise reduction, adaptive scaling, morphological operations — with deep hierarchical feature extraction compels the model to learn *continuous contextual boundaries* on high-variance inputs rather than fitting to the surface textures of clean training images. The key theoretical claim, which this thesis borrows directly, is that augmentation performs an implicit form of distributional coverage expansion: by presenting the model during training with inputs that span the range of geometrically plausible perturbations, the resulting decision boundaries are robust to distributional shifts that would otherwise move test inputs off the clean-data manifold.

For this thesis, the augmentation argument takes the following specific form. Clean Google Fonts specimens are programmatically subjected to elastic warping, Gaussian blur and noise, edge compression, and randomized inter-character tracking variation — perturbations calibrated to simulate the topology-disrupting deformations characteristic of diffusion model text generation. The theoretical justification, supplied by Plastropoulos and Tegos (2024), is that training on degradation-simulated data reshapes the metric space so that decision boundaries between font families pass through regions of feature space that are stable under topological perturbation — precisely the perturbation regime that generative AI outputs inhabit. The trained recognizer is then evaluated not on its own augmentation distribution but on genuinely deformed generative outputs, and the claim this training strategy makes is that the representation space induced by degradation-augmented training is shared with the representation space of real deformed glyphs. Whether that claim holds — and to what degree — is one of this thesis's primary empirical questions.

Both Sarker (2021) and Plastropoulos and Tegos (2024) share a limitation equally important to note: neither addresses the typographic domain, and neither examines the specific deformation regime produced by diffusion model text generation. Sarker's examples draw from image classification, natural language processing, and time-series prediction; Plastropoulos and Tegos focus on object detection and action recognition benchmarks. The borrowing is therefore theoretical rather than empirical — the taxonomy and augmentation principles are applied to a new domain under new degradation conditions, and the empirical question of whether they transfer is answered by the experiments of this thesis rather than resolved by the cited literature. That gap between general principles and specific typography application is itself a component of the research contribution this thesis claims.

**Research Gap (Batch A).** The seven sources reviewed in this section collectively define the closed-set, geometrically-rigid paradigm that constitutes the established baseline for font recognition and document text analysis. Wang et al.'s (2015) DeepFont established the domain's evaluation protocol — clean-glyph synthetic training, Top-1/Top-3 accuracy measurement, closed-set classification — that successive systems have adopted without renegotiating the underlying assumptions. Classical OCR achieves near-ceiling performance on anatomically correct text, revealing through that ceiling the load-bearing assumption it cannot relax: that the glyph to be recognized is a topologically faithful instantiation of a designed typeface (Authors of ICMLDE, 2024; IEEE Dataset/Architecture Authors, 2018). The recent migration toward attention-based and self-supervised layout analysis removes geometric rigidity from the localization step without addressing the morphological assumptions embedded in the font-identity step (Wang, Lv, et al., 2025; Sheikh et al., 2024). The deep learning taxonomy and augmentation literature licenses the synthetic-degradation methodology but has not been applied to typographic recognition under generative AI deformation conditions (Sarker, 2021; Plastropoulos & Tegos, 2024). None of these works asks whether font identity can be recovered from a glyph whose topology was not copied from a vector master but generated by a probabilistic latent model — a glyph in which deformation is not a noise channel to be inverted but an irreducible property of the generative process. The sections that follow characterize how that generative process produces its characteristic deformations (Section 2.3), then assemble the open-set rejection, metric embedding, and validation infrastructure required to address it (Sections 2.4–2.6).
