Key Distinction
Technical Background
It is meant to provide the reader with the theoretical and technical foundation and context needed to understand your research. 
Think of it as the bridge between general knowledge and your specific problem statement. 

Outline
Introduction to the Domain
Overview of the subfield (e.g., machine learning, distributed systems, cybersecurity).
Why this area is important.
Core Concepts & Definitions
Algorithms, data structures, mathematical models.
Formal definitions (e.g., complexity classes, probability models).
Theoretical Foundations
Key principles (e.g., supervised vs. unsupervised learning, cryptographic primitives).
Equations or models that underpin your research.
Existing Technologies & Frameworks
Tools, libraries, or architectures (e.g., TensorFlow, Hadoop, blockchain protocols).
How they work and their limitations.
Problem Context
Technical challenges in the field.
Why current solutions are insufficient.
Illustrations
Diagrams of architectures, flowcharts, or conceptual models.

Writing Tips
Clarity first → Avoid jargon unless you define it.
Logical flow → Move from general to specific.
Balance detail → Enough depth to inform, but not so much that it becomes a textbook.
Visual aids → Diagrams, tables, or equations make complex ideas digestible.
Citations → Anchor every major claim in APA style.

Steps in Writing
Steps to Write the Technical Background
Start Broad, Then Narrow Down
Begin with an overview of the field (e.g., machine learning, distributed systems, cybersecurity).
Gradually zoom in on the specific sub-area your thesis addresses.
Example: “Machine learning is a subset of AI… Within ML, convolutional neural networks (CNNs) are widely used for image classification.”
Define Key Concepts
Introduce technical terms, theories, and models that will appear later in your thesis.
Use clear definitions and, where appropriate, equations or diagrams.
Example: “Overfitting occurs when a model performs well on training data but poorly on unseen data.”
Explain Underlying Principles
Cover the scientific or mathematical foundations relevant to your work.
Example: optimization methods, probability theory, or computational complexity
Discuss Existing Technologies/Frameworks
Describe tools, libraries, or systems commonly used in your field.
Highlight their strengths and limitations.
Example: “PyTorch provides dynamic computation graphs, making it flexible for research prototyping.”
Situate the Problem
Explain the technical challenges or gaps that motivate your research.
Example: “Despite CNNs’ success, their computational cost remains a barrier for deployment in resource-constrained environments.”
Use APA Citations Throughout
Cite sources whenever you explain a concept, framework, or prior work.
Paraphrase most of the time, quote sparingly.
Example: “CNNs exploit spatial hierarchies in images, making them more efficient than fully connected networks (LeCun, Bengio, & Hinton, 2015).”
End with a Transition
Conclude by pointing toward your methodology.
Example: “Having established the theoretical foundations of CNNs, the next chapter outlines the specific architecture and training procedure employed in this study.”

APA Rules for Tables
Numbering
Each table is numbered consecutively (Table 1, Table 2, etc.) in the order they appear.
Numbers are bold and placed above the table.
Titles
Provide a clear, concise title in italics, written in title case.
Title goes directly below the table number.
Example:
Table 2
Comparison of CNNs and Traditional ML Methods
Formatting
Tables should be clean and simple — no vertical lines, minimal horizontal lines.
Use consistent font (same as body text, use Arial 12 pt).
Align numbers and text neatly (e.g., decimal points aligned).
Notes
If you need to explain abbreviations, symbols, or provide source information, add a note below the table.
Types of notes:
General note: Explains the table as a whole.
Specific note: Refers to a particular column or row.
Probability note: Indicates statistical significance (e.g., p < .05).
Example:
Note. CNN = Convolutional Neural Network; SVM = Support Vector Machine. Adapted from LeCun, Bengio, & Hinton (2015).

Citations
If the table is adapted from another source, cite it in the note and include the full reference in your reference list.
Example in note: Adapted from Krizhevsky, Sutskever, & Hinton (2012).
Placement
Place tables close to where they are first mentioned in the text.
Always refer to the table in the narrative.
Example: “As shown in Table 2, CNNs outperform traditional ML methods in scalability and accuracy.”

APA Rules for Figures
Numbering
Figures are numbered consecutively (Figure 1, Figure 2, etc.) in the order they appear.
The number is bold and placed above the figure.
Titles (Captions)
Provide a descriptive caption in italics, written in title case.
The caption goes directly below the figure.
Example:
Figure 2
Simplified architecture of a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN).
Formatting
Figures should be clear, high-quality, and easy to read.
Avoid decorative elements — keep it professional and focused on conveying information.
Labels inside the figure should match the text (e.g., “Convolution Layer,” “Pooling Layer”).
Notes
If needed, add a note below the caption to explain abbreviations, symbols, or the source.
Example:
Note. Adapted from Krizhevsky, Sutskever, & Hinton (2012).
Citations
If the figure is adapted or reproduced from another source, cite it in the note and include the full reference in your reference list.
Example in text: “As shown in Figure 2, CNNs process images hierarchically (LeCun, Bengio, & Hinton, 2015).”
Placement
Place figures close to where they are first mentioned in the text.
Always refer to the figure in your narrative.
Example: “Figure 2 illustrates the hierarchical structure of CNNs, highlighting the role of convolutional and pooling layers.”

Clear, professional, no decorative elements.

Notes: Optional, placed below caption (e.g., abbreviations, source info).

Placement: Near first mention in text, always referred to in narrative.
