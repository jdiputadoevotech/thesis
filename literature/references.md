## Batch 1 synthesis — open-set scoring for typographic anomalies (2024–2026)

The current open-set/OOD literature splits the rejection decision into a **score** and a **threshold**, and the recent work clusters by where in the network that score is read. Hofmann et al. (2024) read it as a *free-energy* quantity, treating a high-energy crop as off-manifold, and show that mining boundary-adjacent outliers tightens the known/unknown frontier. Djurisic et al. (2024) and Karunanayake et al. (2025) keep the decision in **logit space** but disagree on the signal: LTS rescales the whole logit vector by a feature-derived scalar before thresholding, while ExCeL argues the *ranking* of the non-top classes carries the discriminative open-set signal — a framing that maps cleanly onto a Top-K font shortlist, where the order of the runner-up fonts is itself diagnostic of a hallucinated glyph. Underneath both score families, Wang et al. (2024) attack the *representation*: a non-negativity constraint disentangles the embedding so that the axes separating known font styles do not entangle with the deformation artefacts that signal an anomaly, which is the precondition for any of the above scores to threshold reliably. Lu et al. (2025) provide the task-oriented scaffolding that ties these post-hoc scores to the training-agnostic deployment this thesis targets.

**Gap for this thesis:** none of these methods are evaluated on *typographic* OOD — probabilistic glyph deformation, micro-warping, variable kerning. They reject natural-image anomalies (texture, semantic novelty), leaving open whether energy/logit-ranking scores transfer to GenAI-induced font hallucination over a localized Google Fonts palette. *(Batch 2 to extend: contrastive metric-learning OOD, synthetic-degradation robustness.)*

## Batch 2 synthesis — ViT / self-supervised backbones for layout-independent style recognition (2024–2026)

Where Batch 1 fixed the *rejection score*, Batch 2 supplies the *representation* it scores over. The recent backbone literature converges on a frozen self-supervised ViT: Oquab et al. (2024) show DINOv2 yields all-purpose features that already separate fine-grained categories under a linear probe with no fine-tuning, which is the cheapest path to embedding a hallucinated glyph crop. But those features are not clean out of the box — Darcet et al. (2024) demonstrate that self-supervised ViTs sprout high-norm artifact tokens in low-information regions and repair them with register tokens, a fix that matters precisely for glyph crops, where the discriminative signal is a few thin strokes on an otherwise empty ground. On top of a clean frozen backbone, Chowdhury et al. (2025) attach class-specific prompts that force attention onto the *unique* discriminative patches — the mechanism that, transplanted to type, would isolate serif terminals and stroke contrast rather than the word being spelled. Li, Li, et al. (2024) close the loop on cost: prompt distillation transfers a teacher into a compact student over *unlabeled* domain images, matching this thesis's constraint of no per-glyph labels on GenAI crops. The layout-independence thread runs through all four — patch-level features and prompt attention are read regardless of word length or kerning. Li, Song, et al. (2025) supply the negative result that justifies the whole enterprise: off-the-shelf VLMs get lost in font recognition, derailed by a Stroop-style semantic interference, so reaching for a generic multimodal model instead of a dedicated texture-metric pipeline is not a viable shortcut.

**Gap for this thesis:** these backbones and prompt methods are validated on natural-image fine-grained domains (birds, cars, food) and on *clean* font benchmarks (Li, Song, et al. render undeformed text); none is tested on probabilistically *deformed* glyphs, and none maps to an open-set, localized Google Fonts palette under metric learning. The open question Batch 3 must address: do frozen DINOv2 + register-clean patch features, read through a metric-learning head, stay separable under the elastic warping / kerning jitter that breaks commercial identifiers — and does the Batch 1 rejection score then transfer onto *that* embedding? *(Batch 3 to extend: deep metric learning losses for open-set style retrieval, synthetic-degradation / elastic-augmentation robustness, SSIM-based typographic distance.)*

## Batch 3 synthesis — the generative source of typographic hallucination (2024–2026)

Batches 1–2 built the *recognizer*; Batch 3 documents the *disease* it has to read — and, crucially, supplies the empirical proof that the disease is endemic, not anecdotal. The anchor measurement is Liu et al. (2024): an uncorrected SDXL pipeline renders text correctly "less than 20%" of the time, so deformed glyphs are the default output of generative typography, not a rare failure. The recent rendering literature then decomposes *how* that deformation arises and where it concentrates. Gillani et al. (2025) localize the mechanism to uncontrolled cross-character attention — adjacent characters smear into one another — which is the diffusion-side origin of exactly the micro-warping and variable kerning this thesis treats as the hallucination signature, and they hand over CER/WER as ready degradation metrics. Du et al. (2025) escalate the same mechanism to multi-instance scenes, where text regions entangle and confuse, and contribute **CVTG-2K**, a stressor catalogue indexed by position, quantity, length, and attribute — a principled menu of the layout conditions under which type degrades, directly transferable to designing the thesis's own algorithmic degradation suite. Zhuang et al. (2025) add the distributional shape of the problem: deformation is worst in the *long tail* (small, rare glyphs), so a font identifier built on clean-glyph priors will fail hardest precisely where generative text is most damaged. Shu et al. (2025) close the batch with **VTPBench/VTPScore**, the evaluation taxonomy that lets these scattered signals — rendering accuracy, CER/WER, scene-text stressors — be cited under one measurement frame.

**Gap for this thesis:** this literature studies deformation only to *suppress* it inside the generator, and evaluates with OCR-centric metrics (CER/WER, did the right characters appear) that are blind to *font identity* — none asks whether the deformed output can still be traced back to a source typeface, and none operates on cropped in-the-wild GenAI text against a fixed open-set palette. That inversion — treating the documented attention-smearing / long-tail deformation as the *input distribution* for an open-set font-style metric, scored with a typographic-distance (SSIM/MSE) rather than character-error measure — is the unoccupied intersection this thesis claims, joining Batch 1's rejection scores and Batch 2's frozen-ViT embeddings to Batch 3's characterized degradation.

## Batch 4 synthesis — validation metrics for deformation-robust, open-set font identification (2024–2026)

Batches 1–3 fixed the recognizer, its embedding, and its input distribution; Batch 4 supplies the *measurement* layer — the two questions this thesis must answer empirically: *how close is a predicted font to the crop* (typographic distance) and *how trustworthy is the Top-K shortlist* (prediction distribution). On the distance side, the recent SSIM literature has abandoned the pixel-aligned assumption that breaks on warped glyphs: Zhang et al. (2024) move SSIM into deep-feature space with attention calibration so the score survives geometric misalignment, and Li et al. (2025) push further into segmentation-embedding space to score whether a *transformed* image preserves source *content structure* — both are exactly the re-render-and-compare operation this thesis's SSIM/MSE typographic distance performs against a deformed crop, and both descend from the SSIM/IQA lineage (Wang, Bovik), lending the chosen metric direct provenance. On the uncertainty side, the conformal-prediction literature reframes Top-K from a raw accuracy count into a *calibrated* set: Shi et al. (2024) use top-k label-rank calibration to emit the smallest class-conditional set that provably contains the truth, and Ding et al. (2025) extend the guarantee to the long tail, where rare classes — here, rare/display typefaces — are otherwise dropped. Jiang et al. (2025) join the two threads inside typography itself: their **L2@k / cos@k** metrics score font fidelity by comparing *top-k classifier probability distributions* over an open-world font set, and do so on the very Storia-AI Google Font Classifier this thesis adopts as its baseline — simultaneously a validation-metric precedent and external confirmation of that baseline's standing.

**Gap for this thesis:** the structural-similarity metrics are validated on natural-image translation/IQA, never on re-rendered glyphs against hallucinated type; the conformal methods are validated on natural and long-tail object classes, never on a font palette; and ControlText's L2@k/cos@k assume an *undeformed* input glyph, not a GenAI-warped crop. None couples a *deformation-robust* structural distance (DeepSSIM/SAMScore-style) with a *calibrated* Top-K font prediction set over a localized open-set Google-Fonts palette. That unified validation protocol — typographic distance (SSIM/MSE) + calibrated Top-K + Serif/Sans/Display/Mono family-confusion matrix — is this thesis's measurement contribution, closing the loop from Batch 1's rejection scores through Batch 2's frozen-ViT embeddings and Batch 3's characterized degradation to a metric framework purpose-built for hallucinated type.

## Batch 5 synthesis — pipeline foundations: text localization, metric-topology, and the classical baselines this thesis inverts (2015–2025)

Where Batches 1–4 assembled the novel core (rejection score, frozen-ViT embedding, characterized degradation, validation metrics), Batch 5 supplies the *upstream plumbing* and the *historical contrast* that a thesis needs to be legible to a committee — it does three jobs at once. **First, text localization / layout isolation:** before any font can be scored, the crop has to be extracted, and the recent document-analysis literature has migrated off rigid geometry onto attention. Wang, Lv, et al. (2025) show a ViT + self-attention framework (**TransTab**) parsing long-range layout structure that fixed CNN receptive fields cannot, and Sheikh et al. (2024) push this to the *unlabeled* regime with **UnSupDLA**, extracting layout masks from self-supervised DINO encoders with pseudo-mask refinement — the same frozen-DINO family Batch 2 adopts, now justified on the *upstream* segmentation step and under the no-label constraint that governs in-the-wild GenAI crops. **Second, the metric-learning and taxonomy scaffolding:** the two 2022 entries (rows 23–24) provide the mathematical backing for mapping distorted inputs to *continuous proximity rankings* — deep feature-topology clustering and softmax-entropy structural-distance optimization — rather than forcing them into hard label buckets, while Sarker (2021) and Plastropoulos & Tegos (2024) give the authoritative discriminative/generative/hybrid taxonomy and the augmentation-forces-robust-boundaries argument this thesis leans on to justify its synthetic-degradation training. **Third, the baselines this thesis is defined against:** the classical anchors are here precisely to be inverted — the 2018 IEEE closed-boundary work and the 98%-accuracy OCR-Tesseract pipeline (Authors of ICMLDE, 2024) excel on *anatomically correct* text with environmental noise but presuppose the rigid glyph geometry that GenAI dissolves, and Wang, Yang, et al. (2015) — **DeepFont** — is the domain-defining font-recognition baseline built on *clean* synthetic glyphs and Top-1/Top-3 accuracy, the exact evaluation protocol this thesis adopts but re-targets from pristine vectors to hallucinated crops. Kondo et al. (2024) close the batch with the peer-reviewed mechanism proof: diffusion latent interpolation *natively* emits unclassified, fluid glyph geometries — independent confirmation, from the generation side, that the deformation this thesis identifies is intrinsic to the model class, not a sampling artifact.

**Gap for this thesis:** these sources are load-bearing but each sits on one side of the divide this thesis straddles — the layout/OCR pipelines (rows 21, 22, 25, 26) localize and digitize text yet stop at *characters*, never asking which *typeface* survived the distortion; the metric-topology and taxonomy references (rows 23, 24, 27, 28) supply general feature-clustering theory validated on non-typographic domains; and the classical anchors (DeepFont, closed-boundary layout) are optimized for the pristine, static glyphs that are the antithesis of the input distribution here. None connects attention-based text isolation → deformation-robust metric embedding → open-set font-identity ranking on a localized Google-Fonts palette. Batch 5 thus frames the thesis by contrast: it borrows the upstream localization (DINO/ViT), the proximity-ranking rationale, and the DeepFont Top-K protocol, but re-points all three at the OOD-hallucinated regime that every classical baseline in this batch was built to exclude.
