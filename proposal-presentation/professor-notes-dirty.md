PRESENTATION NOTES:

1. The Big Picture (Context) – 1 or 2 slides
- Establish the territory. 
- It can be an image or textual.
- In one or two sentences, state why this general topic matters to the industry, society, or academic field right now.
- It’s basically the Rationale of the Study.

2. The Specific Problem (The Conflict) – 2 to 3 slides
- Narrow it down to the exact pain point you are looking at.
    - What is going wrong, costing money, or causing inefficiency? 
- Use a concrete statistic or citation here to prove it's a real issue.
- Focus on 
  - Statement of the Problem 
  - Significance of the Study
  - Scope and Limitation

3. The Gap (What We Don't Know) – 2 to 3 slides
- This is the most critical sentence of your rationale. 
- Explain what current literature or solutions are missing. 
- What is the "blind spot" your study will look into?
- Highlight the most relevant studies in your Related Literature.
- Identify the gaps encountered in each study.

4. Your Solution (The Value Add) – 6 to 12 slides
- Conclude by stating how your thesis intends to fill that exact gap.
- Justify why you are doing the study.

The Gold Standard Rule
• The panel wants to see that you are coachable.
• A student with a flawed proposal who takes feedback
gratefully and intelligently will almost always pass over a
student with a perfect proposal who gets defensive and
stubborn.

Presentation Flow
Chair of the panel will do the ff:
1. Greet everyone and acknowledge the proponents.
2. Introduce the members of the panel.
3. Remind the proponents to introduce themselves.
4. Remind the proponents to start a very short prayer.
5. Remind the proponents of the presentation flow:
• 30 minutes power point presentation
• 40 minutes Q & A
• 20 minutes deliberation
6. Remind the proponents of the Final Defense Rubric:
• Oral Examination (Rubric 4) (50%) - individual rating
• Documentation (Rubric 5) (50%) - group rating
